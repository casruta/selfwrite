# Research: Scene-Setting Detail

## Sources
- Nieman Storyboard: "Reporting and writing scenes" (Lauren Kessler)
- Nieman Storyboard: "Eight journalists on reporting and writing scenes"
- The Open Notebook: "Like Being There: How Science Writers Use Sensory Detail"
- The Open Notebook: "Writing Vivid Scenes from a Distance"
- Fiveable: Scene-setting techniques (Narrative Journalism)

## Key Principles

1. **A scene is not a description.** A scene has setting + characters + something happening. It advances the plot AND reveals character. Exposition explains context; a scene puts the reader inside the moment. (Kessler, Nieman)

2. **The "telling detail" principle.** Select ONE precise detail that carries emotional weight rather than comprehensive description. Kessler's example: a "scarlet bathrobe" communicates domesticity and comfort more powerfully than paragraphs of physical description. The telling detail does double duty: it describes AND characterizes.

3. **Prioritize uncommon senses.** Sight is the default and the weakest scene-setter because cameras do it better. Smell, sound, and touch are what writing can capture that no other medium can. "If it's not in the notebook, it's not in the story." (The Open Notebook)

4. **Extract, don't accumulate.** "Have more material than needed, then extract only what serves the moment." Over-describing is worse than under-describing. Too little = fuzzy picture. Too much = reader overwhelmed. (Kessler)

5. **Over-dramatization is the cardinal sin.** Adding adjectives, adverbs, and metaphors when the moment already carries emotional weight is manipulative. Sparse narration for dramatic moments lets action speak. If the detail is strong enough, it doesn't need decoration.
