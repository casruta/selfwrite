## Key Execution Principles

1. **First sentence earns the second**: The lede's only job is to make the reader want the next paragraph. Tension, mystery, or surprise outperform summary every time. (The Open Notebook)

2. **Lean and mean**: Strip jargon, institution names, technical terms, and unnecessary qualifiers from the first sentence. David Grimm (Science editor): prefer short sentences free of clutter. (The Open Notebook)

3. **One idea, subject-verb-object**: Lead sentence should contain one idea and follow S-V-O structure. Multiple ideas in the first sentence = reader abandonment. (Multiple sources)

4. **Read aloud test**: Read the lede out loud. If you stumble, it's too long or tangled. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite. (Purdue OWL, MasterClass)

5. **Concrete nouns over abstract ones**: "a three-bedroom in Scarborough" beats "a residential property." "Maria Chen" beats "a prospective buyer." Specifics create images; abstractions create glaze. (The Open Notebook)

6. **Evocative word pairing**: Choose unexpected but precise adjectives. Quammen paired "wondrous" and "frangible" to signal importance + vulnerability in two words. (The Open Notebook)

7. **The tension test**: Does the lede present tension, mystery, surprise, or a challenge? If it merely explains, it's a summary, not a lede. (The Open Notebook: "Effective ledes present some form of tension, mystery, surprise, or challenge rather than simply explaining the story.")

8. **25-35 word ceiling**: Hard ledes should not exceed 35 words. Under 30 is better. Over 40 is almost always too long. (Multiple sources agree on this range.)
