# Research: Kicker Endings

## Sources
- The Open Notebook: "Good Endings" (theopennotebook.com)
- Poynter: "Putting Endings First"
- Longreads: "Sticking the Landing: On Kickers"
- Writing Cooperative: "7 Simple Ways To End Articles With Strong Kickers"
- Outdoor Writers Association of America

## Key Principles

1. **"The kicker drives the story into the reader."** While the lede drives the reader INTO the article, the kicker drives the story INTO the reader. It's what echoes after the reader puts the piece down. (OWAA)

2. **Four kicker types:**
   - **Echo/Circle-back**: Returns to the opening scene, person, or image. Creates a "full circle" effect. The strongest type for features. (The Open Notebook)
   - **Quote**: A revealing, pithy final quote. BUT overused to the point of cliché. Ending with a quote "deprives writers and readers of other, more effective ways." (Poynter)
   - **Scene**: A final moment, image, or action that crystallizes the story's meaning without stating it. Show, don't tell.
   - **Forward-looking**: Points to what comes next, asks a question that furthers the conversation, or projects implications. Good for investigations and policy pieces.

3. **"Look back upstream" (John McPhee)**: If you're stuck on an ending, your best kicker may already exist earlier in the piece. Go find it and move it.

4. **The kicker must earn its place.** It should feel like the story's natural culmination, not a tacked-on moral. If it sounds like a thesis statement, it's an editorial comment, not a kicker. (The Open Notebook)

5. **Kicker anti-patterns:**
   - Just stopping (no closure, no resonance)
   - Summarizing what was already said ("In conclusion...")
   - Moralizing ("And that's why we must act now")
   - Default quote ending (lazy, even if the quote is decent)
   - Introducing brand new information that wasn't set up
