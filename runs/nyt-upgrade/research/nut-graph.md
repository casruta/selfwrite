## Key Principles

1. **The nut answers three questions**: (a) What is this story about? (b) Why does it matter? (c) Why now? If your nut doesn't answer all three, it's incomplete. (Pooh Shapiro, WashPost)

2. **Five-sentence formula** (Nieman Storyboard):
   - Sentence 1: The problem (what happened)
   - Sentence 2: The present (where we are now)
   - Sentence 3: The scope (zoom out from anecdote to bigger picture)
   - Sentence 4: The implications (what's at stake, what's next)
   - Sentence 5: Expert voice (quote about larger significance)
   This is a drafting tool, not a rigid template. Final nuts may compress to 2-3 sentences.

3. **Write the nut before the draft** (Erika Check Hayden, science communication professor): "The nut graph is both clarifying for the reader and clarifying for the writer in helping distill down what story they are actually trying to tell."

4. **Positioning is flexible**: Arrives within the first 3-6 paragraphs. After a 1-paragraph hard lede, it's paragraph 2. After a 3-5 paragraph anecdotal lede, it's paragraph 4-6. In a strong narrative, the nut can be implicit rather than spelled out. (David Robson, New Scientist)

5. **The pub test** (David Robson): "What one detail would I drop into the conversation at a pub to stop friends' eyes glazing over?" That detail belongs in the nut.

6. **The lede-nut connection**: The nut must feel like a natural continuation of the lede, not a jarring shift. "Disembodied intros that don't gel with the nut graf" are a common failure. (David Robson)

7. **Common mistake**: Skipping the nut entirely because it feels "painfully obvious." Readers need it. Even if the writer finds it unsurprising, the reader is encountering this story for the first time.
