# Research: Attribution Patterns

## Sources
- American Journalism Handbook: "Quotes and Attribution" (Rodrigo Zamith)
- NPR Training: "If you want people to trust your reporting, attribute your sources"
- Fiveable: "Attribution and quotation usage"
- Sage Journals: "Said or told the reporter?" (Appelman, Bien-Aimé, Morales, 2025)

## Key Principles

1. **"Said" is nearly always right.** It's invisible, neutral, and doesn't editorialize. Alternatives like "claimed," "argued," "insisted" add shade. Use them only when the shade is intentional AND justified by evidence. "Said" can be repeated dozens of times in a story without wearing out. (AJH, NPR)

2. **First mention: full name + credential. Subsequent: last name only.** After several paragraphs without a quote, re-introduce with a brief refresher: "said Zamith, the journalism professor." Never use pronouns for attribution. (AJH)

3. **Attribution goes after the fact, not before.** In print/digital journalism, the information is more important than who said it. "The thaw has accelerated, Petrova said" not "Petrova said the thaw has accelerated." For multi-sentence quotes, insert attribution after the first sentence. (AJH, Fiveable)

4. **Three quote types, used strategically:**
   - **Direct quote**: For opinions, emotions, vivid language. Must be exact words.
   - **Paraphrase**: For factual information that's better in the journalist's words. Still needs attribution.
   - **Partial quote**: Key phrase embedded in the journalist's sentence. "What he called a 'catastrophic miscalculation.'"

5. **Source introduction as micro-scene.** Don't just give name + title. Add one action or telling detail: "She set down her clipboard. 'The numbers don't capture it,' she said." This connects attribution to the scene-setting principles from cycle 9.
