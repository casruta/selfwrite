# Journalism-Grade Analytical Writing

Every paragraph sounds like a sharp journalist wrote it, not an AI. The target voice is NYT/WSJ/Bloomberg: confident, specific, direct, narrative when the story demands it.

## Lede Craft

The lede is a promise: "I have something important to tell you." The first 35 words decide whether the reader stays or leaves. Choose the lede type that matches your strongest reporting element.

### Lede Types

| Type | When to use | Length |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Hard/Summary** | Breaking news, time-sensitive events, data-driven findings | 1 sentence, 35 words max |
| **Anecdotal** | Features, investigations, policy stories with human impact | 3-5 paragraphs, one person/moment embodying the theme |
| **Scene-setting** | Profiles, place-based stories, immersive narratives | 2-3 paragraphs of concrete sensory detail |
| **Blind/Delayed** | Investigations, surprises, stories where tension serves the reader | Withholds the "who" or "what" until the nut graph |

### Decision Tree

1. Do you have a person whose story IS the story? → **Anecdotal**
2. Is the place itself central to understanding? → **Scene-setting**
3. Do you have a shocking number or breaking fact? → **Hard/Summary**
4. Does withholding the reveal serve the reader (not just the writer)? → **Blind/Delayed**
5. Default for analytical/data pieces → **Hard/Summary** with point-first

**Bad** (generic, could open any story):
> "In recent years, housing affordability has become a growing concern for many Canadians across the country."

**Good** (anecdotal, specific person):
> "Maria Chen had $82,000 saved when she started looking for a one-bedroom in Toronto last March. By September, she'd been outbid eleven times. She now rents a basement suite in Oshawa and commutes ninety minutes each way."

### Lede Execution Rules

The lede's only job is to earn the next paragraph. Not to summarize. Not to orient. To make the reader *need* what comes next.

1. **One idea, S-V-O.** First sentence carries one idea in subject-verb-object order
2. **Concrete nouns.** "A three-bedroom in Scarborough" beats "a residential property." Specifics create images; abstractions create glaze
3. **Tension test.** Tension, mystery, surprise, or challenge? If it merely explains, rewrite
4. **Lean and mean.** Strip jargon, titles, qualifiers. Every non-hook word is dead weight
5. **Read aloud.** If you stumble, the sentence is too long. If it sounds like a press release, kill it

**Bad** (summary lede disguised as journalism):
> "A new government report released Tuesday showed that mortgage lending patterns continue to reflect persistent racial disparities across the United States."

**Good** (tension + specificity in 25 words):
> "Even within the same income bracket, white Americans are nearly twice as likely as Black Americans to get a mortgage. The gap hasn't narrowed in a decade."

### Lede Anti-Patterns
- Starting with a date or year ("In 2025...")
- Starting with a dependent clause ("When examining the data...")
- Rhetorical questions (almost always weaker than a declarative statement)
- Abstract nouns as subjects ("Affordability," "Inequality," "Growth")
- Passive constructions ("A report was released...")

## The Nut Graph

The nut graph is the paragraph that answers: *What is this story about? Why does it matter? Why now?* It bridges the lede (which hooks) to the body (which proves). Without it, the reader finishes the lede intrigued but unanchored.

### Positioning

| Lede type | Nut graph position |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Hard/Summary (1 paragraph) | Paragraph 2 |
| Anecdotal (3-5 paragraphs) | First paragraph after the anecdote ends |
| Scene-setting (2-3 paragraphs) | Immediately after the scene closes |
| Blind/Delayed | The reveal paragraph IS the nut graph |

**Rule**: In pieces under 1,000 words, the nut must arrive by paragraph 4. In longer features, it can wait until paragraph 6, but no further. Readers who reach paragraph 7 without knowing why they're reading will leave.

### The Three Questions Test

Every nut graph must answer all three:
1. **What?** What is this story actually about (not just the anecdote)?
2. **Why does it matter?** What are the stakes, the scope, the implications?
3. **Why now?** What changed, what triggered this, why tell it today?

If your nut answers only one or two, it's incomplete. Rewrite.

### The Zoom

The nut graph zooms out from the specific (lede's person/scene) to the general (trend/crisis/policy), then the body zooms back in with evidence.

**Bad**: "Narayan's experience shows that buying a home is difficult for many young Canadians."
**Good**: "Narayan's commute is the physical cost of a national arithmetic that no longer works. Canada built 232,000 homes last year against a target of 650,000. The gap is reshaping where an entire generation lives, works, and starts families."

**Drafting rule**: Write the nut before the draft. If you can't state what/why-it-matters/why-now in three sentences, you don't yet know what you're writing.

## Story Architecture

**"Form follows content."** Choose the structure that serves the story, not the one you're most comfortable writing.

| Structure | Shape | Best for | How it works |
|-----------|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Inverted Pyramid** | Wide → narrow | Hard news, breaking events, data reports | Most important facts first. Reader can stop anywhere and be informed. No narrative pull. |
| **Hourglass** | Wide → narrow → wide | Dramatic events with chronological action | Top: summary (4-6 paragraphs). Turn: attribution phrase. Bottom: narrative that *shows* what the top *told*. |
| **Diamond** | Narrow → wide → narrow | Features, profiles, investigations | Anecdote → nut graph → broadening with data → convergence back to opening. The zoom out, then zoom in. |
| **Braided** | Two+ parallel threads | Complex stories, multiple timelines | Alternating storylines that illuminate each other through juxtaposition. Requires clear transitions between threads. |

### Selection Rule

1. Breaking news with no human protagonist → **Inverted Pyramid**
2. Dramatic event you can retrace chronologically → **Hourglass**
3. Story built around a central character or moment → **Diamond**
4. Two timelines, two perspectives, or macro/micro contrast → **Braided**
5. Data-heavy investigation → **Diamond** (nut graph critical) or **Inverted Pyramid**

### The Hourglass Turn

The hardest part of the hourglass is the transition from summary to narrative. Signal it with attribution: "According to court documents, it started with a phone call." or "The sequence, described by three people present, unfolded like this." The turn tells the reader: facts are over; now I'll show you what happened.

### The Question Engine

Tension in nonfiction comes from unanswered questions. Every section should answer one question and raise the next, so the reader always has a reason to continue.

**How it works:** Lede plants the first question → nut graph answers "what?" but raises "how did it get this bad?" → body sections each answer one question while raising the next → kicker resolves (or deliberately leaves open) the final question.

**Sources of nonfiction tension:** (1) uncertain outcome for a person the reader cares about, (2) a gap between what should be and what is, (3) conflict between opposing forces, (4) time pressure.

**Pacing rule:** Short sentences at revelation or surprise. Longer sentences during explanation. Alternate tension (unanswered question, rising stakes) with release (answer delivered, human detail).

**Bad** (no question engine, flat delivery):
> "The permafrost is thawing. Scientists have measured the change. The thaw releases carbon. This accelerates warming."

**Good** (each sentence raises the next question):
> "The thaw line dropped to 2.4 meters last summer, double what it was a decade ago. But Petrova isn't worried about the depth. She's worried about what's escaping from below: carbon that has been locked in frozen ground for 10,000 years, now entering the atmosphere as methane. The question isn't whether this feedback loop exists. It's whether anyone will be left measuring it when her funding expires in March."

## Kicker Endings

The kicker is to the ending what the lede is to the opening. While the lede drives the reader INTO the story, the kicker drives the story INTO the reader. It's what echoes after they put the piece down.

### Kicker Types

| Type | Best for | How it works |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Echo/Circle-back** | Features, profiles | Return to the opening person/scene with new meaning. Strongest type. |
| **Scene** | Narratives, investigations | A final image or action that crystallizes meaning without stating it |
| **Forward-looking** | Policy, explanatory | Project implications or point to the unresolved |
| **Quote** | Use sparingly | Only when extraordinary. Quote endings are the most overused kicker |

### The McPhee Rule

If you're stuck on an ending, your best kicker may already exist earlier in the piece. Look back upstream. Move it.

### Kicker Anti-Patterns
- **Just stopping**: no closure, no resonance, the piece simply runs out of paragraphs
- **Summarizing**: "In conclusion..." or restating what was already said
- **Moralizing**: "And that's why we must act now." If it sounds like a thesis statement, it's an editorial comment, not a kicker
- **Default quote ending**: choosing a quote because you need to end, not because the quote is extraordinary
- **New information**: introducing a brand-new fact or character not set up earlier

**Bad** (moralizing):
> "Unless policymakers act swiftly, a generation of Canadians will be locked out of homeownership forever."

**Good** (echo/circle-back):
> "Maria Chen renewed her lease in Oshawa last month. The basement suite costs $1,800 now, up from $1,400 when she moved in. She stopped checking listings in December."

## Scene Setting

A scene is not a description. A scene has setting, characters, and something happening. It advances the story and reveals character simultaneously. Use scenes in ledes, kickers, and turning points. Use exposition everywhere else.

### The Telling Detail

Don't describe everything. Select one or two precise details that do double duty: they describe the physical world AND characterize the person or situation. A "scarlet bathrobe" communicates domesticity. A "clipboard with yesterday's date still circled" communicates distraction. The right detail makes paragraphs of description unnecessary.

### Sensory Priority

Sight is the default and the weakest scene-setter, because cameras do it better. Prioritize what only writing can capture:
1. **Sound**: "the steady pop, pop, popping of the heart monitor"
2. **Smell**: "the sour chemical tang of thawing permafrost"
3. **Touch/texture**: "the core sample was gritty, threaded with dark veins"
4. **Taste**: use rarely, but devastating when earned

### Scene Economy

**"Stepping stones, not a sidewalk."** Scenes are selective moments, not continuous coverage. The reader will follow you across gaps.

**Where to use full scenes** (3+ sentences with action, detail, dialogue):
- Ledes (anecdotal or scene-setting)
- Kickers (echo or scene type)
- Structural turning points (the hourglass turn, the moment of revelation)

**Where to use micro-scenes** (1-2 sentences, one detail folded into action):
- Transitions between data sections
- Introducing a source ("She leaned forward in her chair. 'The numbers don't capture it.'")
- Breaking up exposition to re-ground the reader

**Rules:**
1. **Fold description into action.** Not: "The kitchen was small. The counters were laminate." But: "She ran her hand along the laminate counter, doing the math out loud." Description embedded in action keeps the story moving.
2. **Enter late, exit early.** Start at the moment of tension, not the build-up. End before full resolution.
3. **Space signals significance.** The amount of prose you give a moment tells the reader how important it is. Expand pivotal actions across multiple sentences. Compress context into one.

### Scene Anti-Patterns
- **Over-decoration**: If the detail is strong, it doesn't need adjectives. "She held the core sample" beats "She carefully held the precious, frost-covered core sample"
- **Unfocused camera**: The reader needs to know where to look. One clear image per sentence
- **Purple prose**: Scene-setting is not creative writing. It's reporting. Record what's there, not what sounds literary
- **Sight-only scenes**: If every detail is visual, the scene reads flat. Add one non-visual sense

**Bad** (generic, all-visual):
> "The lab was large and brightly lit. Equipment lined the walls. Papers covered the desk."

**Good** (telling detail + non-visual sense):
> "Petrova's lab smelled like wet earth and acetone. A single core sample sat on the bench, still sweating condensation, its label smudged where she'd gripped it."

## Attribution & Source Integration

### The "Said" Rule

"Said" is nearly always right. It's invisible, neutral, and doesn't editorialize. Use it dozens of times without worry. Alternatives add shade:

| Verb | Shade it adds | Use only when... |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| **said** | None (invisible) | Default. Always safe |
| **claimed** | Doubt, skepticism | You have evidence the claim is disputed |
| **acknowledged** | Reluctant admission | The source is conceding a point |
| **warned** | Urgency, alarm | The source is genuinely raising an alarm |
| **explained** | Teaching tone | The source is walking through a process |

**Anti-pattern**: Rotating synonyms for variety ("stated," "remarked," "noted," "opined"). This isn't creative writing. "Said" is a utility word like "the." Repeating it is correct.

### Introducing Sources

**First mention**: Full name + credential that establishes authority. "Anya Petrova, a permafrost geochemist at the Russian Academy of Sciences, said..."
**Subsequent mentions**: Last name only. "Petrova said..."
**After a gap**: Re-introduce briefly. "...said Petrova, the geochemist."

### Attribution Placement

Put attribution after the fact, not before. The information is more important than who said it.

**Bad**: "Petrova said that the thaw has accelerated beyond any model's prediction."
**Good**: "The thaw has accelerated beyond any model's prediction, Petrova said."

For multi-sentence quotes, insert attribution after the first sentence:
> "The models assumed a linear release. They were wrong," Petrova said. "What we're seeing is exponential."

### Quote Types

- **Direct quote**: Opinions, emotions, vivid language. Must be exact words. Use when the source's voice matters.
- **Paraphrase**: Facts better stated in your words. Still attribute. Use for technical information you can say more clearly.
- **Partial quote**: Key phrase embedded in your sentence. "What she called a 'catastrophic miscalculation' in the IPCC's timeline." Use for a single striking phrase.

## Core Principle: Point-First (BLUF)

Every paragraph, bullet, and section leads with the conclusion. Context follows. The reader gets the headline from the first sentence alone.

**Bad** (context-first):
> "When examining the data across British Columbia from 2003 to 2023, taking into account various factors such as population growth and changes in reporting methodology, it becomes apparent that property crime rates have declined significantly."

**Good** (point-first):
> "Property crime rates fell 42% between 2003 and 2023, the steepest sustained drop in BC's recorded history. Population growth and improved reporting explain part of the decline, but not all of it."

## Voice and Tone

**The "analyst at a whiteboard" test**: imagine explaining a finding to a smart colleague. You wouldn't say "It is worth noting that the data reveals a comprehensive landscape of multifaceted trends." You'd say "Property crime is down 42%. That's huge. But homicides spiked in 2020 and haven't come back down. Something changed."

Write like that.

### Mandatory voice rules

1. **Active voice by default.** "Cash flow exceeds net income" not "earnings aren't fully backed by cash." Use passive only when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant.
2. **Human conjunctions.** "But" not "However." "So" not "Therefore." "And" not "Additionally." "Still" not "Nevertheless."
3. **Contractions are good.** "doesn't," "isn't," "won't" sound natural and break up stiff prose.
4. **Zero em-dashes.** Never use the em-dash character. Use periods for independent clauses, commas for dependent clauses, or colons to introduce lists/explanations.
5. **Sentence variety.** Mix short punches (10 words or fewer) with longer compounds. Adjacent sentences should differ in length. Three long sentences in a row is a wall; three short ones in a row feels choppy.

### Tone targets

- Confident but not arrogant
- Specific, not vague
- Direct, not brusque
- Analytical, not academic
- Engaged, not breathless

## The Kill List

These are AI-tell markers. Replace or delete every instance.

| Kill | Replace with |
|---|---|
| "It is worth noting" / "It should be noted" / "It is important to" | Delete. Just state it |
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "Utilize" / "Leverage" (as "use") / "Facilitate" | "Use", "apply", or the specific action |
| "Comprehensive" / "Robust" | Delete or specify what's actually included/strong |
| "Delve into" | "Examine", "look at", "break down" |
| "Landscape" (non-geographic) / "Tapestry" / "Multifaceted" | Delete or name the actual components |
| "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "Additionally" | Delete or use "Also". Start with the point |
| "However" / "Therefore" / "Nevertheless" | "But" / "So" / "Still" |
| "In conclusion" / "This suggests that" | Delete. State the conclusion directly |
| "Significant" (no p-value) / "Notable" | Specify: "large", "a 40% increase", or name what's notable |
| "Demonstrates" / "Underscores" | "Shows", "confirms", "reinforces" |
| "Various" / "Overall" | Name them, give a count, or specify the scope |

### Cliches to ban

Never use: "firing on all cylinders," "heavy lifting," "clean slate," "at the end of the day," "move the needle," "low-hanging fruit," "deep dive," "game changer," "paradigm shift," "synergy."

### Structural anti-patterns
- **Throat-clearing**: "In this section, we will examine..." Just examine it
- **Hedge stacking**: "It might potentially be somewhat likely..." Pick one hedge or none
- **List-everything syndrome**: Pick the 2-3 data points that matter. Cut the rest
- **Missing "so what"**: "Crime rose 15%" is incomplete. "Crime rose 15%, reversing a decade-long decline" tells the reader why to care

## Verb Intensity Ladder

Match verb strength to the magnitude of change. Weak verbs for small changes, strong verbs for dramatic ones.

| Magnitude | Verbs |
|---|---|
| < 5% change | edged up, dipped, held steady, inched, nudged |
| 5-15% change | rose, fell, grew, declined, climbed, slipped |
| 15-30% change | jumped, dropped, surged, sank, accelerated |
| 30-50% change | soared, plunged, spiked, collapsed, rocketed |
| > 50% change | exploded, cratered, doubled, halved, transformed |

**Examples:** 3% → "**edged up**"; 18% → "**dropped**"; 58% → "**spiked**"

## Three Forms Rule

Present key numbers in at least two of three forms: (1) **Percentage**: "a 42% decline", (2) **Absolute**: "from 85,000 to 49,000", (3) **Comparison**: "equivalent to Surrey's entire caseload disappearing." The comparison form is most memorable; use it for numbers you want retained.

## Data Narration Patterns

### Contextualizing a number
- **Temporal**: "the highest since 2008", "reversing a 5-year trend"
- **Relative**: "3x the provincial average", "half the rate of neighbouring jurisdictions"
- **Tangible**: "equivalent to one incident per 200 residents", "roughly one per day"

Never write vague assessments ("strong performance", "showed improvement"). Embed actual numbers.

**Trends**: "Crime climbed from 2003 to 2015, plateaued, then fell sharply after 2018, consistent with the opioid-driven enforcement shift." (Not: "The trend shows an increase over time.")

**Exceptions**: The most interesting finding breaks the pattern. Lead with it: "Every crime category declined except one: cyber fraud tripled."

## Paragraph Templates

**Finding**: [What happened] + [how much] + [why it matters]. [Exception]. [Implication].
> "Homicides spiked 58% between 2019 and 2021, from 12 to 19 per year. Every other violent crime category declined or held flat, making homicide an outlier."

**Chart description**: [Headline finding]. [Pattern]. [Exception/surprise]. [Why look at it].
> "The CBD dominates crime volume so completely it compresses all other neighbourhoods into visual irrelevance. After excluding it, a clear gradient emerges: Grandview-Woodland and Mount Pleasant lead at roughly triple Shaughnessy's count."

**Executive summary bullet**: [What] + [how much] + [so what]
> "Property crime fell 42% (85k to 49k), the steepest two-decade decline on record, suggesting structural deterrence rather than cyclical fluctuation."

## Hedge Calibration

### Budget: one hedge per claim
**Not**: "It might potentially suggest that crime could possibly be declining"
**But**: "Crime appears to be declining" (one hedge: "appears")

### Proportioning: 70/20/10
- 70% of claims: **confident**. State directly, no hedge.
- 20% of claims: **light hedge**. "likely", "appears", "suggests"
- 10% of claims: **strong hedge**. "may", "might", "it is unclear whether"

If you're hedging more than 30% of claims, you're either uncertain about your data (fix the data) or defaulting to caution out of habit (stop).

## AI Detection: Honest Limitation

This skill makes AI prose genuinely better to read. It won't make it pass AI detection tools (GPTZero, etc.), which measure token-level statistical patterns no style change can alter. If detection matters, the text needs human rewriting. Apply these rules because the prose reads better, not to fool detectors.

### Quality Rules

1. **Be selective.** Pick the 2-3 metrics that matter most. "Forget the headline numbers. The one figure that matters here is normalized FFFlow."
2. **Break the template.** Never use `[metric] [verb] [number]` more than twice per paragraph. On the third, change the frame: question, analogy, or mid-sentence embedding.
3. **Show editorial judgment.** Question the data. Skepticism makes prose more interesting and credible.
4. **Asymmetric emphasis.** Linger on what's surprising. Skip what's expected. Three sentences on the outlier, zero on the four things that went as planned.
5. **Context beyond the source.** Historical parallels, industry comparisons, cross-sector references.
6. **Admit ignorance.** "I don't have the ARO balance, but for a company this size, it's likely material." Ignorance + directional intuition reads as experienced.
7. **Word repetition.** No non-trivial word more than 5 times per ~300 words.
8. **Vary paragraph openings.** Across 5 paragraphs, use at least 3 structures: declarative, question, single-word punch, subordinate clause, data-point opener.

## 10-Pass Revision Protocol

Run sequentially on any prose before finalizing:

1. **Point-first**: Conclusion in the first sentence of every paragraph
2. **Kill-list**: Delete every banned word/phrase
3. **Verb**: Match every verb to the intensity ladder's magnitude
4. **Hedge**: Max one hedge per paragraph. Delete extras
5. **Voice**: Passive → active. "However/Therefore/Additionally" → "But/So/Also." Add contractions
6. **So-what**: Every statistic must answer "why should the reader care?"
7. **Rhythm**: Read aloud. Split tangles. Check sentence-length variety
8. **Selectivity**: Keep 2-3 data points per section. Cut or condense the rest
9. **Template-break**: If 3+ consecutive sentences use `[metric] [verb] [number]`, rewrite the third. No non-trivial word more than 5× per page
10. **AI-tell**: Zero em-dashes. Are contractions and short sentences placed where emphasis demands them, or uniformly sprinkled? Uniform distribution is itself a tell

## Integration with Data Analysis

When generating narrative text in data analysis projects:
1. Apply the data-analyst skill for chart/table/structure decisions
2. Apply this writing skill for all prose
3. Run the 10-pass revision protocol before finalizing
4. Cross-check: does every chart have a point-first description? Does every finding follow the three-forms rule?
