# Sample 06 — Narrative Arc Tension

Scenario: Feature profile of a climate scientist (even cycle). Exercising: question engine, tension-release pacing, stakes escalation, nonfiction tension sources.

---

The steel rod slides in too easily. Anya Petrova pulls it free, wipes the mud from the depth mark, and checks: 2.4 meters. She writes the number in her field notebook and doesn't say anything for a while.

In 2014, her first summer in Tuktoyaktuk, the same rod stopped at 1.2 meters. The permafrost was solid below that line, frozen for roughly 10,000 years. Now the thaw reaches twice as deep. What took millennia to freeze is unfreezing in a decade.

That would be alarming enough on its own. But the problem isn't the thaw. It's what the thaw releases.

Permafrost stores roughly twice as much carbon as the entire atmosphere, locked into organic matter that never had time to decompose. When the ground warms, microbes wake up and start eating. They exhale methane and CO2. The methane is 80 times more potent as a greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide over a 20-year window. So the warming causes thaw, and the thaw causes more warming. Scientists call it a positive feedback loop. Think of it as an interest rate on a debt nobody agreed to take on. It compounds.

Petrova can measure the compounding. What she can't do is stop it, and soon she may not be able to measure it either. Federal funding for permafrost monitoring fell 18% between 2019 and 2025. Her current grant expires in five months. Three of her five graduate students left last year for industry jobs that pay double. The youngest, a master's student named Kai, told her he couldn't afford Vancouver rent on a stipend.

She's 47. She's published 63 papers. She has measured permafrost on four continents. And she is, for the first time, considering whether the ground she studies will outlast the career she built studying it.
