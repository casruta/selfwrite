# Baseline Sample — Housing Affordability Crisis

---

Canada's housing affordability crisis deepened in 2025, with the average home price reaching $782,000, a 6.3% year-over-year increase that outpaced wage growth by more than three to one. The gap between what Canadians earn and what they'd need to buy a home hasn't been this wide since Statistics Canada began tracking the ratio in 1976.

The numbers are stark. A household earning the national median income of $74,600 can afford a mortgage of roughly $340,000, less than half the average purchase price. In Toronto and Vancouver, the ratio is worse: median earners can cover barely a third of the average home cost.

But the national figures obscure a split that matters more than the headline. In Calgary, Edmonton, and the Prairie cities, prices rose just 2.1%, roughly in line with inflation. The crisis is concentrated in three provinces: Ontario, British Columbia, and, increasingly, Nova Scotia, where interprovincial migration from Toronto pushed Halifax prices up 14.8%, the fastest growth of any Canadian metro.

New housing starts fell 11% nationally in 2025, from 261,000 to 232,000 units. The federal government's target of 3.9 million new homes by 2031 would require starts to triple from current levels. At the present pace, Canada will build roughly 1.4 million units over that period, a shortfall of 2.5 million homes.

The policy response so far hasn't matched the scale of the problem. The First-Time Home Buyer Incentive, launched in 2019, has reached fewer than 25,000 households. Immigration targets remain at 500,000 per year, adding demand that the construction industry can't absorb.
