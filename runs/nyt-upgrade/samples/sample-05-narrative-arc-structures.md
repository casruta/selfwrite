# Sample 05 — Narrative Arc Structures

Scenario: Housing affordability investigation (odd cycle). Exercising: hourglass structure (summary top → turn → chronological narrative), story architecture selection.

---

Canada's housing construction pipeline collapsed in 2025. New starts fell 11% nationally to 232,000 units, the lowest figure since 2016 and less than a third of the 650,000 the federal government says are needed annually. The shortfall will reach 2.5 million homes by 2031 at the current pace. No policy enacted since the crisis began has meaningfully altered the trajectory.

The First-Time Home Buyer Incentive, launched in 2019 with a $1.25 billion commitment, has reached fewer than 25,000 households. The Housing Accelerator Fund, announced in 2022, disbursed its first payments last year but targets municipal zoning, not construction labor. Meanwhile, immigration continues to add 500,000 residents annually, roughly the population of Halifax, into a market that can't house the people already here.

How this gap became an emergency is, in part, the story of one municipal council meeting in Brampton, Ontario, last February.

The council had convened to approve a 4,200-unit subdivision on the city's northern edge, a proposal that had cleared environmental review and secured provincial support two years earlier. The developer, Mattamy Homes, had pre-sold 1,100 units. Families had arranged mortgages.

Then Councillor Jeff Bowman moved to defer. He cited traffic studies. Five councillors voted with him. The subdivision stalled. Mattamy pulled its crew to a project in Arizona, where permits took eleven weeks.

Eight months later, the Brampton site is a field. The 1,100 families are still renting. And the 4,200 units the city needs more than any traffic study will never count toward next year's housing starts.
