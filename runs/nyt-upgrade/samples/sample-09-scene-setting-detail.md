# Sample 09: Scene-Setting Detail
**Topic**: Housing affordability investigation
**Structure**: Diamond (scene lede → nut → data broadening → echo kicker)
**Focus**: Demonstrating telling detail, non-visual sensory elements, scene vs. exposition

---

The open house smells like fresh paint and desperation. Twenty-three people shuffle through a semi-detached in Brampton that was listed Thursday morning and will close by Sunday. The floors creak under the weight of competing buyers. A woman in a parka stands in the galley kitchen, running her hand along the laminate counter, doing the math out loud to someone on speakerphone: mortgage payment, daycare, car. She pauses at the number. Hangs up.

The house is listed at $849,000. In 2019, the same address sold for $480,000. The sellers didn't renovate. They didn't add a bedroom. They waited.

That waiting game is now the dominant investment thesis in southern Ontario's housing market. Homeowners who bought before 2020 have gained an average of $340,000 in equity without lifting a hammer. First-time buyers, meanwhile, need a household income of $175,000 to qualify for a mortgage on a median-priced home in the Greater Toronto Area, up from $98,000 five years ago. The gap between those who own and those who don't has widened into something that looks less like a market correction and more like a caste system.

The federal government's response has been architectural: zoning reform incentives, accelerated permitting, a foreign buyers ban that expired and was quietly renewed. None of it has touched the demand side. Immigration targets remain at 500,000 per year. Construction starts have fallen for three consecutive quarters.

The woman in the parka is back in her car now, engine running, still parked in front of the house. She hasn't left. She's on the phone again.
