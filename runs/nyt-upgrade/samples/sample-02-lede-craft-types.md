# Sample 02 — Lede Craft Types

Scenario: Housing affordability investigation (odd cycle). Exercising: anecdotal lede, lede type selection, first-35-words rule.

---

Priya Narayan had done everything right. Engineering degree from Waterloo, six years at a consultancy pulling $95,000 a year, $114,000 in savings. When she started shopping for a condo in Toronto last April, her mortgage broker told her she could afford $420,000. The median one-bedroom was listed at $635,000.

She was outbid four times before she stopped counting. By December, she'd moved to Kitchener, bought a townhouse for $510,000, and added ninety minutes to her commute in each direction. "I didn't leave Toronto," she said. "Toronto left me."

Narayan's story is one version of a crisis that has reshaped where Canadians live, what they can afford, and how far they're willing to drive to own four walls. Home prices rose 6.3% nationally in 2025, reaching an average of $782,000, a figure that conceals a deeper fracture. In Calgary, prices edged up 2.1%. In Halifax, where Toronto emigrants have flooded the market, they jumped 14.8%, the fastest growth of any Canadian metro.

The shortfall isn't a mystery. Canada built 232,000 homes last year. The federal government's own target calls for 650,000 annually through 2031. At the current pace, the country will fall 2.5 million units short. Every policy lever pulled so far, the First-Time Home Buyer Incentive chief among them, has reached fewer than 25,000 households against a deficit measured in millions.

The math, in other words, doesn't work. And it won't until the country builds at a pace it hasn't sustained since the 1970s.
