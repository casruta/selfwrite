# Sample 11: Attribution Patterns
**Topic**: Housing affordability investigation
**Structure**: Hourglass (summary top → attribution turn → narrative bottom)
**Focus**: Demonstrating multiple sources, "said" rule, quote types, source introduction

---

Canada's housing shortage will take until at least 2060 to close at current construction rates, according to a federal analysis obtained by this reporter, more than three decades longer than the government's public target of 2030.

The document, prepared by Infrastructure Canada's internal modelling unit, projects that at the current pace of 240,000 starts per year, the country will need 36 years to close a deficit the Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation pegs at 3.5 million units. The government's public-facing Housing Plan assumes 500,000 starts annually, a rate Canada has never sustained.

Mike Moffatt, an economist at the Smart Prosperity Institute who advises the federal government on housing, said the gap between the internal projection and the public target isn't a surprise.

"Everyone in the building knows the 2030 number is aspirational," Moffatt said. "The question is whether aspirational targets drive action or just buy time."

He isn't alone. Carolyn Whitzman, a housing policy researcher at the University of Ottawa, called the 2030 target "a political number, not a planning number." She pointed to the construction labor shortage as the binding constraint: Canada has 300,000 fewer trades workers than it needs, and apprenticeship completions have been flat for a decade.

The federal government declined to comment on the internal analysis but pointed to $4 billion in housing accelerator funds disbursed since 2023.

Moffatt leaned back in his chair. "We keep announcing money," he said. "But you can't spend your way past a labor shortage. The bottleneck isn't capital. It's people who know how to frame a wall."
