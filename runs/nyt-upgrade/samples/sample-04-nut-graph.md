# Sample 04 — Nut Graph

Scenario: Feature profile of a climate scientist (even cycle). Exercising: nut graph positioning, three questions test, zoom technique.

---

Anya Petrova spends her summers on a shrinking island. Each June, she flies from her office at the University of British Columbia to Tuktoyaktuk, a hamlet of 900 people on the Arctic coast of the Northwest Territories. The shoreline has retreated 40 meters since she first arrived in 2014. One house has already fallen into the Beaufort Sea. Two more will follow before next spring.

She measures what's disappearing. Her team drives steel rods into the permafrost and records, month by month, how deep the thaw line drops. In 2014, it was 1.2 meters. Last summer, it hit 2.4. "The ground remembers winter shorter than it used to," she says. "It's forgetting."

Petrova's data points compose one small chapter in the largest geological shift since the end of the last ice age. Permafrost underlies 24% of the Northern Hemisphere's land surface, roughly 23 million square kilometers. It stores twice as much carbon as the entire atmosphere. As it thaws, that carbon escapes as methane and CO2, accelerating the warming that caused the thaw. Scientists call this a feedback loop. The rest of us should call it an emergency.

Yet the field studying this emergency is itself endangered. Federal funding for permafrost monitoring fell 18% between 2019 and 2025. Petrova's grant expires in March. Three of her five graduate students have taken industry positions because academic salaries can't match the cost of living in Vancouver. She's 47 and considering leaving the country.

The permafrost doesn't care about funding cycles. It just keeps thawing.
