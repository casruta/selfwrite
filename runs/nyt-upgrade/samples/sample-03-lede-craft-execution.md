# Sample 03 — Lede Craft Execution

Scenario: Housing affordability investigation (odd cycle). Exercising: lede execution rules, tension test, concrete nouns, S-V-O, read-aloud test.

---

The house was worth $340,000 when Priya Narayan's parents bought it in 2003. She grew up there, in a split-level on a quiet street in Brampton, sharing a bedroom with her sister until high school. Last spring, the same house sold for $1.1 million. The buyers were investors from Markham. They plan to rent it out.

Narayan, 28, earns $95,000 a year as an engineer. She has $114,000 in savings. None of it matters. In today's market, she qualifies for a mortgage that covers roughly half the cost of the home she grew up in. So she moved to Kitchener, ninety minutes west, where $510,000 still buys four walls and a yard. She commutes three hours a day.

Her story tracks a national fracture that the averages don't capture. Housing prices rose 6.3% across Canada in 2025, but that figure smooths over a geography of extremes. Calgary edged up 2.1%. Halifax jumped 14.8%, driven almost entirely by transplants from Toronto who found they could buy outright what they couldn't rent back home.

The arithmetic is blunt. Canada built 232,000 homes last year against a federal target of 650,000. At the current pace, the country will fall 2.5 million units short by 2031. The government's signature affordability program, the First-Time Home Buyer Incentive, has reached fewer than 25,000 households in six years. That's not a rounding error. That's a confession that the tools don't match the crisis.
