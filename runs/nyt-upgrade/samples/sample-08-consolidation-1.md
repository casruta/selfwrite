**Topic**: Feature profile of a climate scientist

**Focus**: Full-skill comprehensive test after Wave 1 consolidation

Anya Petrova pulls a core sample from the permafrost shelf and holds it up to the Arctic light. The ice is cloudy, threaded with dark veins of organic matter that hasn't seen the atmosphere in 12,000 years. She marks the depth on her clipboard: 2.4 meters. A decade ago, the thaw line sat at 1.2.

"It's not the depth that keeps me up at night," she says. "It's the smell."

What she's smelling is decomposition. As permafrost thaws, ancient organic material begins to break down, releasing methane and carbon dioxide into an atmosphere already saturated with both. Petrova's research station in Yakutia has documented a 340% increase in methane emissions from the surrounding tundra since 2015. That number makes her site one of the fastest-warming points on Earth, and it makes her work a race against her own findings.

The feedback loop is simple in principle, devastating in practice. Warming thaws permafrost. Thawing releases greenhouse gases. Gases accelerate warming. Think of it as compound interest on a debt nobody agreed to take on. The Arctic holds an estimated 1.5 trillion metric tons of carbon in frozen ground, roughly twice what's currently in the atmosphere. Even a partial release would overwhelm every emissions reduction target on the books.

But Petrova's funding expires in March. Her team of four has been cut to two. The Russian government withdrew logistical support in 2024, and Western grant agencies won't fund fieldwork in Yakutia without a diplomatic framework that doesn't exist.

She seals the core sample in a sterile container and labels it with a date she isn't sure anyone will read. The thaw line will be deeper next summer. She already knows where.
