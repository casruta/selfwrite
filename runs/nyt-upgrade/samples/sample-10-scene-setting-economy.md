# Sample 10: Scene-Setting Economy
**Topic**: Feature profile of a climate scientist
**Structure**: Diamond (scene lede → nut → broadening → micro-scenes → echo kicker)
**Focus**: Demonstrating scene economy: full scenes at structural moments, micro-scenes to break exposition

---

The helicopter sets down on a gravel bar in the Kolyma River basin and Anya Petrova is already unbuckling. She has nine hours of daylight and fourteen sampling sites. The rotors haven't stopped when she drops onto the tundra, boots sinking into ground that should be frozen in October but isn't.

She isn't here to confirm what she already knows. She's here because the models are wrong. Her station's methane readings have exceeded every projection for three consecutive years, and the gap between observed emissions and modeled emissions is widening, not narrowing. If Petrova's data holds, the Arctic permafrost isn't a slow-release carbon source. It's an accelerating one.

The distinction matters enormously. The IPCC's most recent assessment assumes permafrost will release carbon gradually through 2100, a manageable addition to the global budget. Petrova's field data suggests a nonlinear curve: emissions doubling roughly every eight years at her site. Extrapolate that across the 23 million square kilometers of Arctic permafrost and the math breaks every net-zero pledge on paper.

She kneels at the first site and drives a steel corer into the ground. It slides in farther than it should. She marks the depth.

"Two point seven meters," she says to no one, recording the number on a clipboard already warped from moisture. Last year it was 2.4. The year before, 1.9.

At the fourteenth site, she pulls the corer out and holds it up. The bottom six inches are unfrozen mud, black and sharp-smelling, alive with decomposition. She seals it, labels it, and radios for pickup. The helicopter won't come for another hour. She sits on the gravel bar and watches the river, which is running brown with sediment that used to be locked in the bank.
