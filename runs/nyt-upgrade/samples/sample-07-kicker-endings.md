# Sample 07: Kicker Endings
**Topic**: Housing affordability crisis in Canada
**Structure**: Diamond (anecdote → nut → data → echo kicker)
**Focus**: Demonstrating echo/circle-back kicker that drives the story into the reader

---

James Okafor moved to Hamilton in 2021 with a plan that made sense on paper: commute to Toronto, save on rent, buy in five years. He found a two-bedroom walkup on Barton Street for $1,350 a month. The math worked.

Then Hamilton's market caught up.

The city that was supposed to be Toronto's affordable alternative isn't anymore. Average home prices in Hamilton hit $788,000 last year, triple what they were in 2015. The gap between Hamilton and Toronto has narrowed from $400,000 to $140,000. For buyers earning under $100,000, the difference between the two cities is now purely theoretical: neither is reachable.

This isn't a Hamilton story. It's the story of every escape valve in southern Ontario closing at once. Kitchener, London, Barrie, St. Catharines: each followed the same curve, just 12 to 18 months behind Toronto. Economists call it the "price shadow," the ripple effect of a core market pushing demand outward. But there's nowhere left to ripple to. The last affordable metro within commuting distance of a major job center crossed the $500,000 threshold in 2024.

The federal government's housing accelerator fund has disbursed $4 billion since its launch, enough to incentivize permits for roughly 100,000 units. Canada needs 3.5 million by 2030. At the current pace, it will take until 2058 to close the gap.

Okafor's rent on Barton Street is $2,100 now. He stopped talking about the five-year plan sometime last winter. He doesn't commute to Toronto anymore, either. The company went remote, then laid off his division in March. He's looking at Sudbury.
