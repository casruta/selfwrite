## Cycle 12: Evidence Weaving

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "Does each piece of evidence resolve a tension the reader already feels, or does it arrive uninvited?" (The narrative-need test. If the reader isn't already wondering about the answer, the evidence is an interruption. Setup creates the question; evidence answers it. This is the question engine applied to sourcing.)
- "Would the reader lose something if this quote were paraphrased?" (The quote-value test. If the answer is no, paraphrase. Quote only when the source's exact words carry meaning you can't reproduce: "functionally fictional," "measuring with a ruler and the thing is moving in leaps.")

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **The setup-evidence-significance pattern is the evidence-level version of the question engine.** At the story level, the question engine creates tension across paragraphs. At the evidence level, setup-evidence-significance creates tension within a paragraph. Both work by the same mechanism: create a need, then fill it. This unification suggests that all good journalism reduces to the same structure at every scale.
- **Never quote routine facts.** This is a non-obvious rule that beginners and AI both violate constantly. "The temperature was 30 degrees, Petrova said" wastes the reader's attention on a quote that adds no voice. The test: does the quote contain opinion, emotion, or language more vivid than the journalist could produce? If not, paraphrase.
- **Corroboration from a second source is evidence architecture.** Shakhova's "three other Siberian stations" doesn't just add credibility; it transforms Petrova's finding from a single data point into a pattern. The second source doesn't need to say something new; it needs to change the evidentiary weight of what the first source said.

**Scoring Insights:**
- evidence_integration reached 9. The 8→9 jump required not just multiple sources (cycle 11 achieved that) but strategic integration: every quote and data point woven into narrative need, quote/paraphrase decisions visibly strategic, data braided with quotes rather than stacked. 9 requires invisible integration; 8 only requires competent multi-source citation.
- +0.15 is the smallest keep-gain yet. Diminishing returns confirmed. The remaining 5 dimensions at 8 will each need a genuinely new technique, not just refinement of existing ones, to reach 9.

## Cycle 11: Attribution Patterns

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "How many named sources does this piece have? If it's one, you don't have journalism; you have a profile." (The source-count test. Two named sources is the minimum for a reported piece. Three is where evidence integration starts to feel braided. Transferable to any analytical writing: a single data source is an observation; multiple sources are evidence.)
- "Does each source add a distinct perspective, or are they saying the same thing in different words?" (The source-diversity test. Moffatt framed the problem as aspirational vs. real. Whitzman identified the binding constraint. Each source earned their place by adding something the other didn't.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **"Said" is a utility word like "the."** Rotating attribution verbs for variety ("stated," "remarked," "noted") is a common AI tell because AI has been trained to avoid repetition. Humans use "said" repeatedly because they know it's invisible. Using "said" consistently is actually an anti-AI signal.
- **Attribution placement reveals editorial judgment.** "The thaw has accelerated, Petrova said" (fact first) treats the information as primary. "Petrova said the thaw has accelerated" (source first) treats the source as primary. Journalism defaults to fact-first because the reader wants the information, not the attribution. The exception: when the source's credibility IS the news.
- **Source braiding is a narrative architecture technique.** Moffatt frames → Whitzman echoes → government responds → Moffatt caps. This isn't just multi-source citation; it's dialogue. The sources interact with each other's ideas even though they were interviewed separately. This produces narrative architecture AND evidence integration simultaneously.

**Scoring Insights:**
- **ALL DIMENSIONS AT 8.** Second convergence milestone (first was all-7 at cycle 6). Attribution was the single highest-leverage remaining intervention because it directly addressed BOTH lagging dimensions (voice_authority and evidence_integration) simultaneously.
- The +0.35 gain matches cycle 9's +0.35, making Wave 2 significantly more productive than expected. The skill had genuine gaps (scenes, sources) that Wave 1's structure-focus couldn't address. This validates the 4-wave design: structure first, then craft, then polish, then harden.
- 10 kept, 0 reverted. Still no reverts. This can't continue. Expect the first revert in cycles 12-17 as additions become more incremental.

## Cycle 10: Scene-Setting Economy

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "Is this a full scene, a micro-scene, or exposition? And is that the right choice for this moment's significance?" (The scene-weight audit. Forces deliberate allocation of narrative space. A depth reading that IS the discovery deserves expansion; a helicopter ride doesn't. Transferable to any writing where you're deciding how much space to give each element.)
- "If I cut this scene to one sentence, what would I keep?" (The compression test for scene economy. The answer reveals the telling detail. Everything else is decoration.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **"Stepping stones, not a sidewalk" is the single best metaphor for scene economy.** The reader will follow you across gaps. You don't need to show every step. This applies far beyond scenes: it applies to data presentation (skip the middle of the trend, show the endpoints), to argument structure (don't prove every link in the chain), to transitions (the reader can infer the connection).
- **Micro-scenes are the secret weapon of data journalism.** A single sentence of action between data paragraphs re-grounds the reader in the physical world. "She kneels at the first site and drives a steel corer into the ground. It slides in farther than it should." This isn't scene-setting for immersion. It's scene-setting as pacing: a human moment that breaks the abstraction of numbers.
- **Space signals significance is the economy version of asymmetric emphasis.** The quality rules already say "linger on what's surprising." Scene economy operationalizes this: give a pivotal measurement four sentences, give a helicopter ride zero. The reader unconsciously reads more space as more importance.

**Scoring Insights:**
- narrative_architecture jumped to 8 because the sample now has VISIBLE scene structure: full scene → exposition → micro-scene → exposition → full scene. The architecture isn't just logical (diamond, hourglass); it's textural (scene density varies by importance). This is a new dimension of "architecture" the rubric measures but the skill didn't previously address.
- Two dimensions remain at 7: voice_authority and evidence_integration. Both have the same root cause: single-source samples. The next cycles (attribution-patterns, evidence-weaving) should directly target these.

## Cycle 9: Scene-Setting Detail

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "Is this a scene or a description? Does it have setting, characters, AND something happening?" (The scene test. A description is static: "The lab was cold." A scene has action: "She gripped the sample, its label smudging under her thumb." If nothing happens, it's not a scene. Transferable to any narrative writing where you need the reader inside the moment.)
- "Which single detail, if removed, would make the reader lose the most?" (The telling detail test. Forces you to find the one image that does double duty: describes AND characterizes. If you can remove a detail without loss, it wasn't telling enough.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **Smell is the most underused and most powerful sense in writing.** "The open house smells like fresh paint and desperation" anchors the reader physically in a way that visual description can't. Sound is second. Cameras capture sight; only writing captures chemical senses. Prioritize non-visual senses for scenes that feel genuinely immersive.
- **"Show mechanism then name it" is a powerful explanation pattern.** The sample showed the house appreciating $369K without renovation, then named the pattern ("the dominant investment thesis"). This sequence (concrete example → abstract label) is more accessible than the reverse. It improved explanatory_clarity from 7 to 8 as a side effect of scene-setting, confirming that good scene-setting and good explanation are not independent.
- **Character action IS the scene.** The woman doing math on speakerphone, pausing at the number, hanging up: three actions that tell a complete emotional story without a single adjective. This is the "show don't tell" principle at its most concrete. The action IS the telling detail.

**Scoring Insights:**
- Scene-setting guidance improved two dimensions simultaneously (lede_quality +1, explanatory_clarity +1). This mirrors the pattern from cycle 3 (lede execution → architecture improvement) and cycle 4 (nut graph → voice improvement). Craft techniques have cascading effects across dimensions because good writing is integrated, not modular.
- The +0.35 gain is the largest since cycle 5 (+0.50). Wave 2 is producing larger gains than expected. Scene-setting was a genuine gap in the skill that structure-focused Wave 1 couldn't address.

## Cycle 8: Consolidation-1

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "If this sentence were deleted, would the reader lose anything they can't get from another sentence?" (The compression test. Applied to every line during consolidation. Transferable to any editing task: if a sentence is redundant with another, one of them must die.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **Merged kill-list entries are more memorable than individual ones.** "Furthermore / Moreover / Additionally → Delete or use Also" is easier to recall and apply than three separate rows. Grouping by the replacement action (delete, simplify, specify) makes the kill list a faster editorial tool.
- **Compression itself is a quality improvement.** The 321-line file is clearer than the 395-line file not because content was lost but because redundancy was removed. Duplicate guidance creates ambiguity about which rule to follow. Single authoritative statements are clearer.
- **19% compression with zero quality loss is possible when sections grow organically.** Organic growth creates duplication (throat-clearing appeared in two anti-pattern lists, hard lede example appeared in two places). Periodic consolidation is essential after content-addition phases.

**Scoring Insights:**
- Comprehensive scoring confirms all dimensions at 7+ after compression. The compressed skill produces the same Petrova climate scientist sample quality as the uncompressed version. This validates the compression: no signal was lost.
- **Checkpoint memory persisted.** Three memory files created: craft principles (feedback), anti-patterns (feedback), journalism sources (reference). These will inform future sessions without re-reading the full learnings log.

## Cycle 7: Kicker Endings

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "Does the ending drive the story into the reader, or does it just stop?" (The kicker test. Transferable to any writing that needs to resonate after the reader puts it down. A report's conclusion, a memo's final recommendation, a presentation's last slide. If the ending merely summarizes, it hasn't done its job.)
- "Does the last paragraph make the reader see the opening differently?" (The echo test. If the ending returns to the opening scene/person and the reader sees it with new understanding, the piece has completed its arc. This is the strongest signal of narrative coherence.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **The echo/circle-back kicker is the highest-leverage ending technique.** It doesn't require new information, a great quote, or a forward prediction. It only requires returning to the opening with new meaning. This means the ending is set up in the first paragraph, not invented at the last one. The implication for drafting: write the lede and kicker as a pair before writing the body.
- **Ironic displacement is a specific echo pattern.** When the ending mirrors the opening but with diminished expectations (Hamilton→Sudbury, $1,350→$2,100, five-year plan→stopped talking about it), the reader feels the story's weight without being told to feel it. This is "show, don't tell" at the structural level.
- **The kicker anti-patterns are almost all forms of writer intrusion.** Summarizing, moralizing, editorializing: each is the writer telling the reader what to think. A good kicker lets the reader arrive at the conclusion themselves. This principle applies beyond endings to any moment where the writer is tempted to explain what the evidence already shows.

**Scoring Insights:**
- anti_ai_quality is the first dimension to break 7. The echo kicker is a technique that AI rarely produces because it requires the writer to plan the ending from the opening. Most AI-generated text ends with either a summary or a forward-looking platitude. A deliberate callback to a specific person's changed circumstances is a strong human-writing signal.
- Diminishing returns have arrived: +0.10 is the smallest gain yet (prior cycles averaged +0.40). This confirms the learnings from cycle 6: we're in polish mode. Cycle 8 consolidation should focus on compression and cross-referencing, not new structural additions.

## Cycle 6: Narrative Arc Tension

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "At the end of each paragraph, does the reader have an unanswered question pulling them into the next one? If not, you've broken the chain." (The question engine audit. Transferable to any long-form writing. A chain of questions is the simplest model for sustaining engagement.)
- "Is this section tense because it's fast, or because something is unresolved?" (Distinguishes pacing from tension. Speed is a tool, not the goal. A slow, deliberate paragraph can be more tense than a rapid one if the stakes are high enough.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **The question engine is the single most portable journalism technique.** It works for features, investigations, explanatory pieces, data narratives, reports, memos, emails. Every piece of writing that asks "why should the reader continue?" benefits from explicit question-planting.
- **Analogy-then-mechanism is a two-pass explanation pattern.** The permafrost sample explained the feedback loop first via analogy ("interest rate on a debt nobody agreed to take on") then via mechanism ("microbes wake up and start eating. They exhale methane"). The analogy makes it accessible; the mechanism makes it precise. This pattern improved explanatory_clarity from 6 to 7.
- **Genuinely varied pacing produces anti-AI quality as a side effect.** "It compounds." (2 words) after a 30-word analogy paragraph isn't a style trick. It's the natural landing of an idea that needs emphasis. When pacing variation emerges from content rather than from a rule ("sprinkle short sentences"), it reads as human because the rhythm serves the meaning.

**Scoring Insights:**
- ALL DIMENSIONS NOW AT 7. This is the convergence signal 6 threshold. Future cycles enter "polish mode": sentence-level refinement, not structural changes. The foundation is complete. Waves 2-4 should add texture (scenes, sources, tone, transitions) and test the skill adversarially, not add more structural guidance.
- The run has maintained a +0.25 to +0.50 gain per cycle with zero reverts. This is unsustainable; expect diminishing returns and the first revert in Wave 2 as additions become more incremental.

## Cycle 5: Narrative Arc Structures

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "What structure does this story's content demand? Are you choosing a structure or defaulting to one?" (Forces intentional architecture selection. Most AI-generated text defaults to inverted pyramid or flat data stacks. Choosing hourglass, diamond, or braided is itself a quality signal.)
- "Can the reader stop after paragraph 3 and still be informed?" (The hourglass test. If the answer is yes AND the story still rewards reading to the end, the structure is working on both levels.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **"Form follows content" is the master principle.** Every structural decision should start here. A dramatic chronological event demands hourglass. A character-driven story demands diamond. Forcing the wrong structure onto a story weakens every dimension simultaneously.
- **The hourglass's chronological narrative embeds evidence as plot points.** In the council-vote sample, "pre-sold 1,100 units" and "environmental review cleared" aren't data citations. They're stakes in a story. This is how evidence integration jumps from adequate to seamless: make data part of the plot, not an interruption.
- **Explanatory clarity improves when you show a specific mechanism.** Abstract explanations ("bureaucratic barriers slow construction") score low. Concrete mechanisms ("one council vote killed 4,200 units; the developer left for Arizona") score high. The hourglass narrative naturally produces mechanism-level explanation because chronological storytelling shows cause and effect.

**Scoring Insights:**
- Three dimensions gained +1 simultaneously (narrative_architecture, evidence_integration, explanatory_clarity). This is the biggest single-cycle gain yet. Story architecture is the highest-leverage intervention so far because structure shapes how evidence is presented AND how explanations land.
- All dimensions now at 6+. Approaching convergence signal 6 ("all dimensions at 7+ → enter polish mode"). Two dimensions remain at 6: explanatory_clarity and anti_ai_quality.

## Cycle 4: Nut Graph

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "Can you state in three sentences what this story is about, why it matters, and why now? If not, you don't yet know what you're writing." (Erika Check Hayden's drafting test. Transferable to any writing task: reports, memos, pitches, emails. If you can't nut-graph it, you don't understand it.)
- "What one detail would you drop at a pub to stop your friends' eyes glazing over?" (David Robson's pub test. Forces you to find the single most compelling fact. Transferable to presentations, executive summaries, anything where attention is scarce.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **Write the nut before the draft.** This flips the intuitive order (draft then discover the point). But it clarifies the story for the writer, not just the reader. When the nut is vague, the body will meander. When it's sharp, everything else falls into line.
- **The nut performs a "zoom."** It pulls back from the specific (lede's person/scene) to the general (trend/crisis/policy), then the body zooms back in with evidence. This zoom is the fundamental structural move of narrative journalism. Without it, the lede's anecdote stays isolated, a human-interest garnish on top of data.
- **Nut graph positioning depends on lede type, not on story length.** A hard lede puts the nut in paragraph 2. An anecdotal lede puts it at the end of the anecdote (paragraph 4-6). The rule isn't "early" or "late." It's "immediately after the hook completes its work."

**Scoring Insights:**
- Nut graph improved voice_authority (+1) as a side effect. The zoom forced editorial judgment: stating "The rest of us should call it an emergency" requires taking a position, which reads as authorial voice. Structural guidance produces voice improvement without voice-specific rules.
- explanatory_clarity (5) is now the clear laggard. Three cycles focused on structure (lede, nut, architecture) haven't touched it. Need a dedicated explanatory-depth cycle.

## Cycle 3: Lede Craft Execution

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "Does this first sentence present tension, mystery, surprise, or a challenge? If it merely explains, it's a summary, not a lede." (The Open Notebook's tension test. Transferable to any opening: emails, reports, pitches.)
- "Can you read this opening aloud without stumbling?" (The read-aloud test catches clutter that silent reading misses. Applies to any paragraph, not just ledes.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- **Concrete nouns create images; abstract nouns create glaze.** "A three-bedroom in Scarborough" triggers a mental picture. "A residential property" triggers nothing. This principle extends far beyond ledes: every sentence benefits from concrete specifics over abstractions.
- **Sentence-rhythm variety is the strongest anti-AI signal.** Sample 03 scored +1 on anti_ai_quality because "None of it matters." (4 words) appeared next to a 28-word compound. The key: short sentences must land where *emphasis demands them*, not at regular intervals. Uniform distribution of short punches is itself an AI tell.
- **Execution rules compound with type selection.** Cycle 2's type taxonomy told us to pick anecdotal. Cycle 3's execution rules told us HOW to write the first sentence of that anecdote (S-V-O, one idea, concrete nouns). The combination produced a much stronger lede than either rule alone.

**Scoring Insights:**
- Lede execution improvements cascaded: better ledes also improved narrative_architecture (+1) because a scene-setting opening naturally creates a person→context→data arc instead of a flat data stack. Investing in the opening pays dividends downstream.
- anti_ai_quality improved not from adding anti-AI rules but from the *side effects* of good craft (varied rhythm, specific details). Quality and anti-AI are correlated, not independent.

## Cycle 2: Lede Craft Types

**Expert Questions That Worked:**
- "Does your opening earn the reader's next 10 seconds, or does it assume attention?" (Transferable to any writing: the first sentence is an audition, not a preamble.)
- "What is the single strongest element in your reporting — a person, a number, or a place? Does your lede lead with it?" (Forces lede type selection instead of defaulting to hard news.)

**Craft Principles Discovered:**
- The lede type decision should be driven by the **strongest reporting element**, not by habit or genre convention. A data story can open with an anecdote if the anecdote is stronger than the number.
- 35-word ceiling for hard ledes is a genuine craft standard across multiple J-school sources, not an arbitrary rule. Forces precision.
- Anecdotal ledes need a specific person with specific numbers. "A young professional" is vague. "Priya Narayan, $95,000 salary, $114,000 saved, outbid four times" is journalism.

**Scoring Insights:**
- Adding lede guidance improved voice_authority (+1) as a side effect — the anecdotal opening forced more natural, less analytical prose in the test sample. Lede type influences overall voice.
