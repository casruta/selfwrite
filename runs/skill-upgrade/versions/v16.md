# Journalism-Grade Analytical Writing

Every paragraph sounds like a sharp journalist wrote it, not an AI. The target voice is NYT/WSJ/Bloomberg: confident, specific, direct, narrative when the story demands it.

### The NYT Standard

From the NYT Manual of Style: "The goal is clear, precise, literate prose that effectively conveys important information to busy readers. Those readers should not be misled by error, distracted by sloppiness or annoyed by pedantry, polemic, slang, jargon or heedless incivility. In fact, the only time they should notice our writing at all is if, occasionally, they pause to admire it."

This is the bar: prose invisible in execution, admirable on inspection. Not correct prose. Not clear prose. Prose that disappears into the reader's understanding and only surfaces when they stop to appreciate it.

### Genre Calibration

| Genre | Lead style | Structure | Evidence | Voice |
|-------|-----------|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Hard news/data report** | Summary lede, point-first | Inverted pyramid or hourglass | Numbers front-loaded, 3-forms rule | Direct, authoritative, spare |
| **Feature/profile** | Anecdotal or scene-setting | Diamond or braided | Scenes + quotes + data woven | Immersive, character-driven, tonal range |
| **Opinion/op-ed** | Thesis in first 2 sentences | Argument → evidence → counterargument → conclusion | Crisp evidence: named sources, specific data, policy examples | Confident, committed, persuasive |
| **Explainer** | Hard lede with the finding, then layered explanation | Topic → mechanism → implication | Analogies + data + expert paraphrase | Teaching without condescending |
| **Investigation** | Blind/delayed or scene-setting | Diamond with document reveals | Documents + sources + timeline reconstruction | Controlled tension, let facts carry weight |

### Audience Profiles

Once you know who's reading, apply the corresponding profile:

| Audience | Avg sentence length | Jargon | Active voice | Contractions | Evidence type | Paragraph length |
|----------|-------------------|--------|-------------|-------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **General public** | 12-15 words | None. Define all terms | 90%+ | Yes, naturally | Stories, analogies, contextualized numbers | 2-3 sentences |
| **Policy analysts** | 14-16 words | Domain-specific OK | 70% | Rare | Research findings, cost-benefit, precedent | 4-5 sentences |
| **Business / C-suite** | 15-18 words | Industry standard only | 80% | Rare | Metrics, ROI, competitive positioning | 2-3 sentences, bullets OK |
| **Journalists** | 13-16 words | None. Translate everything | 85%+ | Yes | Facts, quotes, newsworthiness | 2-3 sentences |
| **Academic / specialist** | 20-25 words | Expected | 60% | Never | Peer-reviewed, original data, statistical | 5+ sentences |

**Register consistency**: once you pick a register (formal, semi-formal, informal), maintain it throughout. Shifting from contracted informal prose to un-contracted formal prose mid-piece breaks reader trust.

## Before Writing: The Intake

Before applying any rules, establish what you're writing. The answers to these questions override defaults throughout the skill.

1. **Who is reading?** Experts tolerate jargon and dense information. General audiences need analogies and lower density. Know which you're writing for
2. **What's the purpose?** Inform → lead with findings, complete coverage. Persuade → lead with thesis, handle objections. Explain → lead with analogy, layer toward mechanism
3. **What genre?** News, feature, opinion, explainer, investigation. Each has different structure, evidence, and voice requirements (see Genre Calibration table above)
4. **What tone?** Match the publication. Name a model if possible ("write this like a Bloomberg brief" vs. "write this like a New Yorker feature")
5. **What's the one thing?** In one sentence, what must the reader take away? If you can't state it, you're not ready to write

If no intake is done, default to: general audience, informative purpose, explainer genre, conversational-but-authoritative tone.

## Lede Craft

The lede is a promise: "I have something important to tell you." The first 35 words decide whether the reader stays or leaves. Choose the lede type that matches your strongest reporting element.

### Lede Types

| Type | When to use | Length |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Hard/Summary** | Breaking news, time-sensitive events, data-driven findings | 1 sentence, 35 words max |
| **Anecdotal** | Features, investigations, policy stories with human impact | 3-5 paragraphs, one person/moment embodying the theme |
| **Scene-setting** | Profiles, place-based stories, immersive narratives | 2-3 paragraphs of concrete sensory detail |
| **Blind/Delayed** | Investigations, surprises, stories where tension serves the reader | Withholds the "who" or "what" until the nut graph |

### Decision Tree

1. Do you have a person whose story IS the story? → **Anecdotal**
2. Is the place itself central to understanding? → **Scene-setting**
3. Do you have a shocking number or breaking fact? → **Hard/Summary**
4. Does withholding the reveal serve the reader (not just the writer)? → **Blind/Delayed**
5. Default for analytical/data pieces → **Hard/Summary** with point-first

**Bad** (generic, could open any story):
> "In recent years, housing affordability has become a growing concern for many Canadians across the country."

**Good** (anecdotal, specific person):
> "Maria Chen had $82,000 saved when she started looking for a one-bedroom in Toronto last March. By September, she'd been outbid eleven times. She now rents a basement suite in Oshawa and commutes ninety minutes each way."

### Lede Execution Rules

The lede's only job is to earn the next paragraph. Not to summarize. Not to orient. To make the reader *need* what comes next.

1. **One idea, S-V-O.** First sentence carries one idea in subject-verb-object order
2. **Concrete nouns.** "A three-bedroom in Scarborough" beats "a residential property." Specifics create images; abstractions create glaze
3. **Tension test.** Tension, mystery, surprise, or challenge? If it merely explains, rewrite
4. **Lean and mean.** Strip jargon, titles, qualifiers. Every non-hook word is dead weight
5. **Read aloud.** If you stumble, the sentence is too long. If it sounds like a press release, kill it

**Bad** (summary lede disguised as journalism):
> "A new government report released Tuesday showed that mortgage lending patterns continue to reflect persistent racial disparities across the United States."

**Good** (tension + specificity in 25 words):
> "Even within the same income bracket, white Americans are nearly twice as likely as Black Americans to get a mortgage. The gap hasn't narrowed in a decade."

### Lede Anti-Patterns
- Starting with a date or year ("In 2025...")
- Starting with a dependent clause ("When examining the data...")
- Rhetorical questions (almost always weaker than a declarative statement)
- Abstract nouns as subjects ("Affordability," "Inequality," "Growth")
- Passive constructions ("A report was released...")

## The Nut Graph

The nut graph is the paragraph that answers: *What is this story about? Why does it matter? Why now?* It bridges the lede (which hooks) to the body (which proves). Without it, the reader finishes the lede intrigued but unanchored.

### Positioning

| Lede type | Nut graph position |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Hard/Summary (1 paragraph) | Paragraph 2 |
| Anecdotal (3-5 paragraphs) | First paragraph after the anecdote ends |
| Scene-setting (2-3 paragraphs) | Immediately after the scene closes |
| Blind/Delayed | The reveal paragraph IS the nut graph |

**Rule**: In pieces under 1,000 words, the nut must arrive by paragraph 4. In longer features, it can wait until paragraph 6, but no further. Readers who reach paragraph 7 without knowing why they're reading will leave.

### The Three Questions Test

Every nut graph must answer all three:
1. **What?** What is this story actually about (not just the anecdote)?
2. **Why does it matter?** What are the stakes, the scope, the implications?
3. **Why now?** What changed, what triggered this, why tell it today?

If your nut answers only one or two, it's incomplete. Rewrite.

### The Zoom

The nut graph zooms out from the specific (lede's person/scene) to the general (trend/crisis/policy), then the body zooms back in with evidence.

**Bad**: "Narayan's experience shows that buying a home is difficult for many young Canadians."
**Good**: "Narayan's commute is the physical cost of a national arithmetic that no longer works. Canada built 232,000 homes last year against a target of 650,000. The gap is reshaping where an entire generation lives, works, and starts families."

**Drafting rule**: Write the nut before the draft. If you can't state what/why-it-matters/why-now in three sentences, you don't yet know what you're writing.

## Story Architecture

**"Form follows content."** Choose the structure that serves the story, not the one you're most comfortable writing.

| Structure | Shape | Best for | How it works |
|-----------|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Inverted Pyramid** | Wide → narrow | Hard news, breaking events, data reports | Most important facts first. Reader can stop anywhere and be informed. No narrative pull. |
| **Hourglass** | Wide → narrow → wide | Dramatic events with chronological action | Top: summary (4-6 paragraphs). Turn: attribution phrase. Bottom: narrative that *shows* what the top *told*. |
| **Diamond** | Narrow → wide → narrow | Features, profiles, investigations | Anecdote → nut graph → broadening with data → convergence back to opening. The zoom out, then zoom in. |
| **Braided** | Two+ parallel threads | Complex stories, multiple timelines | Alternating storylines that illuminate each other through juxtaposition. Requires clear transitions between threads. |

### Selection Rule

1. Breaking news with no human protagonist → **Inverted Pyramid**
2. Dramatic event you can retrace chronologically → **Hourglass**
3. Story built around a central character or moment → **Diamond**
4. Two timelines, two perspectives, or macro/micro contrast → **Braided**
5. Data-heavy investigation → **Diamond** (nut graph critical) or **Inverted Pyramid**

### The Hourglass Turn

The hardest part of the hourglass is the transition from summary to narrative. Signal it with attribution: "According to court documents, it started with a phone call." or "The sequence, described by three people present, unfolded like this." The turn tells the reader: facts are over; now I'll show you what happened.

### The Question Engine

Tension in nonfiction comes from unanswered questions. Every section should answer one question and raise the next, so the reader always has a reason to continue.

**How it works:** Lede plants the first question → nut graph answers "what?" but raises "how did it get this bad?" → body sections each answer one question while raising the next → kicker resolves (or deliberately leaves open) the final question.

**Sources of nonfiction tension:** (1) uncertain outcome for a person the reader cares about, (2) a gap between what should be and what is, (3) conflict between opposing forces, (4) time pressure.

**Pacing rule:** Short sentences at revelation or surprise. Longer sentences during explanation. Alternate tension (unanswered question, rising stakes) with release (answer delivered, human detail).

**Bad** (no question engine, flat delivery):
> "The permafrost is thawing. Scientists have measured the change. The thaw releases carbon. This accelerates warming."

**Good** (each sentence raises the next question):
> "The thaw line dropped to 2.4 meters last summer, double what it was a decade ago. But Petrova isn't worried about the depth. She's worried about what's escaping from below: carbon that has been locked in frozen ground for 10,000 years, now entering the atmosphere as methane. The question isn't whether this feedback loop exists. It's whether anyone will be left measuring it when her funding expires in March."

## Information Flow

Derived from Halliday & Hasan's cohesion theory and Prague School functional sentence perspective. These rules govern how information chains across paragraphs and sections.

### Thematic Progression Patterns

Choose the chaining pattern that serves your purpose:

| Pattern | How it works | Best for |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Linear** | End of sentence A becomes start of sentence B | Building momentum, cause-effect chains, discovery narratives |
| **Constant** | Same subject across multiple sentences, new predicate each time | Deep analysis of one entity, profiles |
| **Split-rheme** | One idea branches into multiple sub-topics | Introducing sections of a long piece, survey articles |

**Linear progression** is the journalist's default. It creates the feeling of one fact leading inevitably to the next:
> "The city's water system was built in 1887. That system leaked 40% of its supply by 2020. The leak rate forced a $2 billion infrastructure overhaul."

### Paragraph-to-Paragraph Flow

**The head-to-tail echo.** End a paragraph with a word or concept. Start the next paragraph with that same word (or a synonym). The reader crosses the gap without a transition word.

**Bad** (signposted transition):
> "...the housing market collapsed in 2008."
>
> "Furthermore, the collapse affected the banking sector."

**Good** (head-to-tail echo):
> "...the housing market collapsed in 2008."
>
> "The collapse cascaded through the financial system within weeks."

No "furthermore" needed. The echo word *is* the transition.

### Section Bridges

For breaks between major sections, use one of these (never "In addition" or "Turning to"):

1. **Temporal pivot**: "Three weeks later..." / "By the time the mayor arrived..."
2. **Scene cut**: white space + a new scene that shares a thematic thread with the previous section
3. **Question bridge**: end one section with an implicit question, open the next with its answer

### Information Density

Each sentence should introduce roughly one new idea. If a sentence contains three new facts, split it.

**Overloaded** (4 new ideas):
> "The report, which analyzed 47 countries over 12 years and found policy effectiveness varied by region, GDP, and implementation method, suggests stronger regulations produced 23% faster decarbonization."

**Unpacked** (one idea per sentence):
> "The report analyzed 47 countries over 12 years. Policy effectiveness varied by three factors: region, GDP, and implementation method. Countries with stronger regulations decarbonized 23% faster."

### Scene-to-Data Bridges

The hardest transition in analytical writing: moving from a human story to statistical evidence. Don't signpost it ("Turning to the data..."). Instead:

1. **End the scene with a gap**: "But no one knew the full scope yet."
2. **Use a section break** (white space or subheading)
3. **Open with specific data**: "In 2019, the EPA released figures showing..."

The section break signals the mode shift. Readers expect the perspective to widen. Without the break, the jump from personal to statistical feels jarring.

For the reverse (data → scene): end the data section with an implication that demands a human face, then cut to the person living that implication.

### The Curiosity Loop

Each paragraph should answer one question the reader holds from the previous paragraph and create one or two new questions. The reader can't stop because there's always an open loop.

This is the paragraph-level application of the question engine from Story Architecture. If a paragraph answers a question without opening a new one, the reader has permission to stop. Don't give them that permission until the kicker.

## Kicker Endings

The kicker is to the ending what the lede is to the opening. While the lede drives the reader INTO the story, the kicker drives the story INTO the reader. It's what echoes after they put the piece down.

### Kicker Types

| Type | Best for | How it works |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Echo/Circle-back** | Features, profiles | Return to the opening person/scene with new meaning. Strongest type. |
| **Scene** | Narratives, investigations | A final image or action that crystallizes meaning without stating it |
| **Forward-looking** | Policy, explanatory | Project implications or point to the unresolved |
| **Quote** | Use sparingly | Only when extraordinary. Quote endings are the most overused kicker |

### The McPhee Rule

If you're stuck on an ending, your best kicker may already exist earlier in the piece. Look back upstream. Move it.

### Kicker Anti-Patterns
- **Just stopping**: no closure, no resonance, the piece simply runs out of paragraphs
- **Summarizing**: "In conclusion..." or restating what was already said
- **Moralizing**: "And that's why we must act now." If it sounds like a thesis statement, it's an editorial comment, not a kicker
- **Default quote ending**: choosing a quote because you need to end, not because the quote is extraordinary
- **New information**: introducing a brand-new fact or character not set up earlier

**Bad** (moralizing):
> "Unless policymakers act swiftly, a generation of Canadians will be locked out of homeownership forever."

**Good** (echo/circle-back):
> "Maria Chen renewed her lease in Oshawa last month. The basement suite costs $1,800 now, up from $1,400 when she moved in. She stopped checking listings in December."

## Scene Setting

A scene is not a description. A scene has setting, characters, and something happening. It advances the story and reveals character simultaneously. Use scenes in ledes, kickers, and turning points. Use exposition everywhere else.

### The Telling Detail

Don't describe everything. Select one or two precise details that do double duty: they describe the physical world AND characterize the person or situation. A "scarlet bathrobe" communicates domesticity. A "clipboard with yesterday's date still circled" communicates distraction. The right detail makes paragraphs of description unnecessary.

### Sensory Priority

Sight is the default and the weakest scene-setter, because cameras do it better. Prioritize what only writing can capture:
1. **Sound**: "the steady pop, pop, popping of the heart monitor"
2. **Smell**: "the sour chemical tang of thawing permafrost"
3. **Touch/texture**: "the core sample was gritty, threaded with dark veins"
4. **Taste**: use rarely, but devastating when earned

### Scene Economy

**"Stepping stones, not a sidewalk."** Scenes are selective moments, not continuous coverage. The reader will follow you across gaps.

**Where to use full scenes** (3+ sentences with action, detail, dialogue):
- Ledes (anecdotal or scene-setting)
- Kickers (echo or scene type)
- Structural turning points (the hourglass turn, the moment of revelation)

**Where to use micro-scenes** (1-2 sentences, one detail folded into action):
- Transitions between data sections
- Introducing a source ("She leaned forward in her chair. 'The numbers don't capture it.'")
- Breaking up exposition to re-ground the reader

**Rules:**
1. **Fold description into action.** Not: "The kitchen was small. The counters were laminate." But: "She ran her hand along the laminate counter, doing the math out loud." Description embedded in action keeps the story moving.
2. **Enter late, exit early.** Start at the moment of tension, not the build-up. End before full resolution.
3. **Space signals significance.** The amount of prose you give a moment tells the reader how important it is. Expand pivotal actions across multiple sentences. Compress context into one.

### Scene Anti-Patterns
- **Over-decoration**: If the detail is strong, it doesn't need adjectives. "She held the core sample" beats "She carefully held the precious, frost-covered core sample"
- **Unfocused camera**: The reader needs to know where to look. One clear image per sentence
- **Purple prose**: Scene-setting is not creative writing. It's reporting. Record what's there, not what sounds literary
- **Sight-only scenes**: If every detail is visual, the scene reads flat. Add one non-visual sense

**Bad** (generic, all-visual):
> "The lab was large and brightly lit. Equipment lined the walls. Papers covered the desk."

**Good** (telling detail + non-visual sense):
> "Petrova's lab smelled like wet earth and acetone. A single core sample sat on the bench, still sweating condensation, its label smudged where she'd gripped it."

## Attribution & Source Integration

### The "Said" Rule

"Said" is nearly always right. It's invisible, neutral, and doesn't editorialize. Use it dozens of times without worry. Alternatives add shade:

| Verb | Shade it adds | Use only when... |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| **said** | None (invisible) | Default. Always safe |
| **claimed** | Doubt, skepticism | You have evidence the claim is disputed |
| **acknowledged** | Reluctant admission | The source is conceding a point |
| **warned** | Urgency, alarm | The source is genuinely raising an alarm |
| **explained** | Teaching tone | The source is walking through a process |

**Anti-pattern**: Rotating synonyms for variety ("stated," "remarked," "noted," "opined"). This isn't creative writing. "Said" is a utility word like "the." Repeating it is correct.

### Introducing Sources

**First mention**: Full name + credential that establishes authority. "Anya Petrova, a permafrost geochemist at the Russian Academy of Sciences, said..."
**Subsequent mentions**: Last name only. "Petrova said..."
**After a gap**: Re-introduce briefly. "...said Petrova, the geochemist."

### Attribution Placement

Put attribution after the fact, not before. The information is more important than who said it.

**Bad**: "Petrova said that the thaw has accelerated beyond any model's prediction."
**Good**: "The thaw has accelerated beyond any model's prediction, Petrova said."

For multi-sentence quotes, insert attribution after the first sentence:
> "The models assumed a linear release. They were wrong," Petrova said. "What we're seeing is exponential."

### Quote Types

- **Direct quote**: Opinions, emotions, vivid language. Must be exact words. Use when the source's voice matters.
- **Paraphrase**: Facts better stated in your words. Still attribute. Use for technical information you can say more clearly.
- **Partial quote**: Key phrase embedded in your sentence. "What she called a 'catastrophic miscalculation' in the IPCC's timeline." Use for a single striking phrase.

### Weaving Evidence

Evidence should flow into the narrative, not interrupt it. The reader should never feel the story stop for a citation.

**The setup-evidence-significance pattern:**
1. A sentence that creates a need (a question, a claim, a tension)
2. The evidence that fills it (quote, data, or document)
3. The journalist's "so what" (interpretation, implication, next question)

**Bad** (evidence dumped without setup):
> Petrova published a paper in Nature Geoscience in 2024. The paper found that methane emissions had increased 340%. This is a large increase.

**Good** (evidence woven into narrative need):
> Petrova's team expected the thaw to follow the IPCC's gradual curve. It didn't. Methane emissions at her site jumped 340% between 2015 and 2024, she reported in Nature Geoscience, a rate that makes the IPCC's carbon budget "functionally fictional," as she put it.

**When to quote vs. paraphrase:**
- Quote when the source's exact words are more vivid than yours ("functionally fictional")
- Paraphrase when you can state the fact more clearly than the source did
- Never quote routine facts. "The temperature was 30 degrees, Petrova said" wastes a quote on a number

## Core Principle: Point-First (BLUF)

Every paragraph, bullet, and section leads with the conclusion. Context follows. The reader gets the headline from the first sentence alone.

**Bad** (context-first):
> "When examining the data across British Columbia from 2003 to 2023, taking into account various factors such as population growth and changes in reporting methodology, it becomes apparent that property crime rates have declined significantly."

**Good** (point-first):
> "Property crime rates fell 42% between 2003 and 2023, the steepest sustained drop in BC's recorded history. Population growth and improved reporting explain part of the decline, but not all of it."

**When to break point-first**: Braddock's research found professional writers use explicit opening topic sentences only ~13% of the time. Point-first is the safe default for analytical prose and data writing. But in features, scene-setting ledes, and narrative passages, the controlling idea can be implied or delayed for effect. Break the rule when delay creates tension that serves the reader, not the writer.

### Paragraph as a Unit of Thought

A paragraph should develop one controlling idea through a chain of increasingly specific sentences (Christensen's "subordinate sequence"). Two diagnostic patterns:

- **Coordinate**: topic sentence + parallel examples at the same level. Useful for lists but feels choppy in narrative
- **Subordinate**: each sentence narrows or deepens the previous one. Creates momentum and depth. Preferred for analytical prose

If your paragraph feels flat, check whether it's coordinate when it should be subordinate.

## Voice and Tone

**The "analyst at a whiteboard" test**: imagine explaining a finding to a smart colleague. You wouldn't say "It is worth noting that the data reveals a comprehensive landscape of multifaceted trends." You'd say "Property crime is down 42%. That's huge. But homicides spiked in 2020 and haven't come back down. Something changed."

Write like that.

### Mandatory voice rules

1. **Active voice by default.** "Cash flow exceeds net income" not "earnings aren't fully backed by cash." Use passive only when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant.
2. **Human conjunctions.** "But" not "However." "So" not "Therefore." "And" not "Additionally." "Still" not "Nevertheless."
3. **Contractions are good.** "doesn't," "isn't," "won't" sound natural and break up stiff prose.
4. **Zero em-dashes.** Never use the em-dash character. Use periods for independent clauses, commas for dependent clauses, or colons to introduce lists/explanations.
5. **Sentence variety.** Mix short punches (10 words or fewer) with longer compounds. Adjacent sentences should differ in length. Three long sentences in a row is a wall; three short ones in a row feels choppy.

### Tone targets

- Confident but not arrogant
- Specific, not vague
- Direct, not brusque
- Analytical, not academic
- Engaged, not breathless

### The Voice Modulator (Roy Peter Clark, Poynter)

Voice isn't mysterious. It's the sum of specific choices across these dimensions. Set each dial based on the genre and intake answers:

| Dimension | Dial low | Dial high | News default | Feature default | Opinion default |
|-----------|----------|-----------|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| Language | Concrete, plain | Abstract, sophisticated | Low | Mid | Mid-high |
| Person | Third person (he, she, it) | First person (I, we, you) | Low | Mid | High |
| Metaphor | Sparse, literal | Dense, figurative | Low | Mid-high | Mid |
| Sentences | Short, simple | Long, complex | Short-mid | Varied | Varied |
| Objectivity | Neutral, detached | Opinionated, committed | Low | Mid | High |
| Conventions | Expected, traditional | Experimental, boundary-pushing | Low | Mid | Mid |

**The authenticity test**: read it aloud. Does the voice amplify the subject, or compete with it for attention? If you hear yourself performing cleverness, it's self-indulgent. If you hear clear thinking about the material, it's authentic.

**Voice vs. style**: Style is technique (sentence structure, word choice). Voice is perspective (how you see the world, what you emphasize). You can borrow style. You can't borrow voice.

### Clarity Checklist (what editors actually mark)

Run this before finalizing any piece. These are the most common editor corrections at major publications:

1. **Pronoun antecedents**: every "this," "it," "they" must have a clear referent within 2 sentences. If ambiguous, name the thing
2. **Buried verbs**: if the action is hidden in a nominalization ("made a decision" instead of "decided"), unbury it
3. **Ambiguous parsing**: read each sentence and check if it could be parsed two ways. If yes, restructure
4. **Complexity balance**: short sentences aren't inherently better. Complex nominals ("the administration's decision to postpone the vote") pack information efficiently. The goal is clarity, not brevity. A dense 25-word sentence that's clear beats three choppy 8-word sentences
5. **Tightening**: remove every word that carries no information. But don't strip away rhythm or voice in the process

## Explanatory Craft

Making complex topics accessible without dumbing them down. Grounded in Pinker ("The Sense of Style"), Gentner's analogy research, and science journalism practice (Ed Yong, Upshot, FiveThirtyEight).

### The Zoom Technique

Alternate between abstract (principle) and concrete (example). Most writers don't zoom in enough.

- **After every abstract claim, zoom in**: not "companies like Netflix" but "in 2007, Netflix was mailing 500 red envelopes daily; by 2010, it had 3 million streaming subscribers"
- **After concrete detail, zoom out**: connect the example to the broader pattern or principle
- **Target ratio**: roughly 60% concrete (stories, numbers, scenes) to 40% abstract (principles, frameworks)
- **Rule**: if you have two consecutive abstract paragraphs, you've gone too high. Ground it

### Layered Explanation

For complex topics, build understanding in layers: analogy → mechanism → implication.

1. **Analogy**: relate the unfamiliar to something the reader knows. "Think of antibiotics as precision weapons aimed at bacteria's cell walls"
2. **Mechanism**: explain how it actually works. "They disrupt the protein synthesis that bacteria need to build those walls"
3. **Implication**: so what? "This is why resistance develops: bacteria that survive have walls the antibiotics can't target"

**Rule**: never deploy an analogy without stating its limits. "In this way, X is like Y, but unlike Y, X also..."

### Jargon Management

1. **Can it be removed?** If a common English word means the same thing, use it
2. **If essential**: define on first use in simple words, add two concrete examples, then use the term freely after
3. **Inline beats parenthetical**: embed definitions into the sentence, don't interrupt with parentheses

**Bad** (parenthetical, condescending):
> "The algorithm uses a sigmoid function (a mathematical curve that maps values between 0 and 1)."

**Good** (inline, natural):
> "The algorithm squeezes all numbers through a sigmoid function, a filter that converts anything into a probability between 0 and 1. A large positive number becomes 0.99. A large negative becomes 0.01."

## Sentence Architecture

Grounded in Gopen & Swan ("The Science of Scientific Writing"), Williams ("Style: Toward Clarity and Grace"), and Pinker ("The Sense of Style"). These aren't style preferences. They're how the brain processes written language.

### The Topic-Stress Principle

Every sentence has two power positions: the **topic** (beginning) and the **stress** (end). The topic tells the reader what the sentence is about. The stress carries the emphasis, the new information, the payoff.

- **Topic position**: put familiar, contextual information here. It links backward to the previous sentence
- **Stress position**: put the new, important information here. This is what the reader remembers

**Bad** (new info at start, old info at end):
> "A 340% increase in methane emissions was what Petrova's team measured at the site."

**Good** (familiar context first, new info at end):
> "Petrova's team measured emissions at the site. What they found was a 340% increase in methane."

### Given-Before-New

Start with what the reader already knows. End with what's new. This creates forward momentum: each sentence's ending becomes the next sentence's starting context.

**Bad** (new info first, disorienting):
> "Regulatory capture explains the pattern. The agency had been staffed by former industry executives since 2019. Enforcement actions dropped 60%."

**Good** (given → new chain):
> "Enforcement actions dropped 60% after 2019. The timing wasn't coincidental: the agency had been staffed by former industry executives since that year. The pattern has a name. Regulatory capture."

### Right-Branching Preference

English processes main-clause-first, modifiers-after. Front-loading qualifiers forces readers to hold unresolved information in working memory.

- **Left-branching** (avoid): "Despite sustained growth in the residential sector, continued low interest rates, and strong demographic tailwinds, housing starts declined."
- **Right-branching** (prefer): "Housing starts declined, despite sustained growth in the residential sector, continued low interest rates, and strong demographic tailwinds."

Rule: if a sentence opens with more than one dependent clause before the main verb, restructure it.

### Dependency Distance

Keep the subject close to its verb. Long gaps between them force working memory to hold the subject while processing intervening material.

**Bad** (12 words between subject and verb):
> "The committee, after reviewing the submissions from all twelve regional offices and consulting with external auditors, recommended changes."

**Good** (subject → verb → modifiers):
> "The committee recommended changes after reviewing submissions from all twelve regional offices and consulting with external auditors."

### Centre-Embedding Limits

Don't nest more than one clause inside another. Each level of embedding adds cognitive load. Two levels is the practical maximum for readable prose.

**Bad** (triple embedding):
> "The report [that the committee [that the minister appointed] produced] recommended changes."

**Good** (unpacked):
> "The minister appointed the committee. Their report recommended changes."

### Parallel Structure

When listing or comparing, match grammatical form. Broken parallelism creates friction.

**Bad**: "The plan called for reducing emissions, more renewable energy, and to phase out coal."
**Good**: "The plan called for reducing emissions, expanding renewable energy, and phasing out coal."

### The Curse of Knowledge

You know what you mean. The reader doesn't. Watch for:
- Jargon introduced without definition
- Logic steps that feel obvious to you but aren't stated
- Pronouns with ambiguous antecedents
- Acronyms used before being spelled out

Test: would a smart reader outside this specific domain follow every sentence on first pass?

## The Kill List

These are AI-tell markers. Replace or delete every instance.

| Kill | Replace with |
|---|---|
| "It is worth noting" / "It should be noted" / "It is important to" | Delete. Just state it |
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "Utilize" / "Leverage" (as "use") / "Facilitate" | "Use", "apply", or the specific action |
| "Comprehensive" / "Robust" | Delete or specify what's actually included/strong |
| "Delve into" | "Examine", "look at", "break down" |
| "Landscape" (non-geographic) / "Tapestry" / "Multifaceted" | Delete or name the actual components |
| "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "Additionally" | Delete or use "Also". Start with the point |
| "However" / "Therefore" / "Nevertheless" | "But" / "So" / "Still" |
| "In conclusion" / "This suggests that" | Delete. State the conclusion directly |
| "Significant" (no p-value) / "Notable" | Specify: "large", "a 40% increase", or name what's notable |
| "Demonstrates" / "Underscores" | "Shows", "confirms", "reinforces" |
| "Various" / "Overall" | Name them, give a count, or specify the scope |

### Cliches to ban

Never use: "firing on all cylinders," "heavy lifting," "clean slate," "at the end of the day," "move the needle," "low-hanging fruit," "deep dive," "game changer," "paradigm shift," "synergy."

### Structural anti-patterns
- **Throat-clearing**: "In this section, we will examine..." Just examine it
- **Hedge stacking**: "It might potentially be somewhat likely..." Pick one hedge or none
- **List-everything syndrome**: Pick the 2-3 data points that matter. Cut the rest
- **Missing "so what"**: "Crime rose 15%" is incomplete. "Crime rose 15%, reversing a decade-long decline" tells the reader why to care

## Verb Intensity Ladder

Match verb strength to the magnitude of change. Weak verbs for small changes, strong verbs for dramatic ones.

| Magnitude | Verbs |
|---|---|
| < 5% change | edged up, dipped, held steady, inched, nudged |
| 5-15% change | rose, fell, grew, declined, climbed, slipped |
| 15-30% change | jumped, dropped, surged, sank, accelerated |
| 30-50% change | soared, plunged, spiked, collapsed, rocketed |
| > 50% change | exploded, cratered, doubled, halved, transformed |

**Examples:** 3% → "**edged up**"; 18% → "**dropped**"; 58% → "**spiked**"

## Three Forms Rule

Present key numbers in at least two of three forms: (1) **Percentage**: "a 42% decline", (2) **Absolute**: "from 85,000 to 49,000", (3) **Comparison**: "equivalent to Surrey's entire caseload disappearing." The comparison form is most memorable; use it for numbers you want retained.

## Data Narration Patterns

### Contextualizing a number
- **Temporal**: "the highest since 2008", "reversing a 5-year trend"
- **Relative**: "3x the provincial average", "half the rate of neighbouring jurisdictions"
- **Tangible**: "equivalent to one incident per 200 residents", "roughly one per day"

Never write vague assessments ("strong performance", "showed improvement"). Embed actual numbers.

**Trends**: "Crime climbed from 2003 to 2015, plateaued, then fell sharply after 2018, consistent with the opioid-driven enforcement shift." (Not: "The trend shows an increase over time.")

**Exceptions**: The most interesting finding breaks the pattern. Lead with it: "Every crime category declined except one: cyber fraud tripled."

## Paragraph Templates

**Finding**: [What happened] + [how much] + [why it matters]. [Exception]. [Implication].
> "Homicides spiked 58% between 2019 and 2021, from 12 to 19 per year. Every other violent crime category declined or held flat, making homicide an outlier."

**Chart description**: [Headline finding]. [Pattern]. [Exception/surprise]. [Why look at it].
> "The CBD dominates crime volume so completely it compresses all other neighbourhoods into visual irrelevance. After excluding it, a clear gradient emerges: Grandview-Woodland and Mount Pleasant lead at roughly triple Shaughnessy's count."

**Executive summary bullet**: [What] + [how much] + [so what]
> "Property crime fell 42% (85k to 49k), the steepest two-decade decline on record, suggesting structural deterrence rather than cyclical fluctuation."

### Variation Targets

Templates are starting points, not formulas. Repeating the same structure across paragraphs is an AI tell.

- In every 5 paragraphs, use at least 3 different lengths: 1-sentence punch, 2-3 sentence transition, 5+ sentence analytical block
- In every 5 paragraphs, use at least 3 different openings: declarative, question, fragment, data-point, subordinate clause
- No two consecutive paragraphs should follow the same template
- Mix 1-sentence kickers with 7-sentence deep dives. Clustering at 4-5 sentences per paragraph is a detection signal

## Hedge Calibration

### Budget: one hedge per claim
**Not**: "It might potentially suggest that crime could possibly be declining"
**But**: "Crime appears to be declining" (one hedge: "appears")

### Proportioning: 70/20/10
- 70% of claims: **confident**. State directly, no hedge.
- 20% of claims: **light hedge**. "likely", "appears", "suggests"
- 10% of claims: **strong hedge**. "may", "might", "it is unclear whether"

If you're hedging more than 30% of claims, you're either uncertain about your data (fix the data) or defaulting to caution out of habit (stop).

## AI Detection & Human-Signal Patterns

AI detectors measure perplexity (word predictability), burstiness (sentence-length variance), subordination density, and lexical diversity. Clean prose from an LLM scores poorly on all four because it lacks the statistical irregularities human writers produce naturally. The rules below address each gap. Apply them because they make prose genuinely better, not to game detectors.

### Quality Rules

1. **Be selective.** Pick the 2-3 metrics that matter most. "Forget the headline numbers. The one figure that matters here is normalized FFFlow."
2. **Break the template.** Never use `[metric] [verb] [number]` more than twice per paragraph. On the third, change the frame: question, analogy, or mid-sentence embedding.
3. **Show editorial judgment.** Question the data. Skepticism makes prose more interesting and credible.
4. **Asymmetric emphasis.** Linger on what's surprising. Skip what's expected. Three sentences on the outlier, zero on the four things that went as planned.
5. **Context beyond the source.** Historical parallels, industry comparisons, cross-sector references.
6. **Admit ignorance.** "I don't have the ARO balance, but for a company this size, it's likely material." Ignorance + directional intuition reads as experienced.
7. **Word repetition.** No non-trivial word more than 5 times per ~300 words.
8. **Vary paragraph openings.** Across 5 paragraphs, use at least 3 structures: declarative, question, single-word punch, subordinate clause, data-point opener.

### Intellectual Authenticity

These markers signal a real mind behind the prose. AI defaults to confident, complete, evenly hedged statements. Humans think on the page.

- **Show your thinking**: "Why does this happen? Three reasons, though the first still surprises me." Let the reader see the reasoning, not just the conclusion
- **Admit gaps with direction**: "I don't have the exact mechanism, but the direction is clear." Ignorance paired with intuition reads as experienced, not uncertain
- **Question your own data**: "The numbers say X, but that doesn't explain Y." Skepticism about your own evidence is the opposite of AI behavior
- **Curiosity markers**: "Which raises a question nobody's asked yet." Genuine wonder, not performative
- **Asymmetric confidence**: be very confident on 70% of claims, lightly hedge 20%, strongly hedge 10%. The mix signals real judgment. Uniform hedging is a tell

### Burstiness

Burstiness measures variance in sentence length. Human writing scores 0.60-1.00. AI-generated text scores 0.15-0.30. This is the single most reliable detection signal.

**Targets:**
- Burstiness coefficient (std dev of sentence lengths / mean) >= 0.50
- Adjacent sentences must differ by 5+ words
- Three consecutive sentences within ±3 words of each other = rewrite one
- In every 5 sentences: at least one <= 8 words, at least one >= 25 words

**Fragment placement:**
Fragments are incomplete sentences used deliberately for emphasis. They work after a complete setup, at narrative turns, and in kickers. Never two fragments in a row.

**Bad** (uniform, burstiness ~0.20):
> "The policy was introduced in 2019. It targeted first-time homebuyers. The program offered subsidized rates. Applications exceeded expectations. The backlog grew steadily."

**Good** (varied, burstiness ~0.65):
> "The policy landed in 2019 with a simple promise: cheaper mortgages for first-time buyers. Applications flooded in. Within six months, the backlog had swallowed the department's entire processing capacity, and nobody in Ottawa seemed to have a plan for what came next."

### Productive Imperfection

Skilled writers break grammatical rules deliberately. AI avoids rule-breaking because it's trained on "correct" text. Strategic imperfection signals a human hand.

| Technique | Frequency | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Sentence fragments** | 1-2 per 500 words | "Wrong." / "Not quite." / "If the data holds." Always after a complete sentence |
| **Conjunction starts** | 1 per 3-4 paragraphs | "But the numbers tell a different story." / "And that's the problem." / "So what changed?" |
| **Comma splices** | Max 1 per 1000 words | "The earnings were off, nobody expected that." Use when the splice creates forward momentum |
| **Rhetorical redundancy** | Max 1 per section | "It's expensive. Very expensive." Deliberate repetition for emphasis |
| **Vague backreferences** | Where context is obvious | "It works. Here's why." The "it" isn't restated because the reader already knows |

**Anti-patterns**: Don't stack imperfections. One fragment followed by a comma splice followed by a conjunction start reads as sloppy, not human. Space them out. Each one should feel earned.

### Rhetorical Devices

AI rarely uses these correctly. When they land, they signal craft.

**Litotes** (understatement via negation): Implies magnitude through restraint. "Not nothing." "No small feat." "Hardly trivial." Use 1-2x per piece for a finding that deserves understatement.

**Zeugma** (one word governing two phrases in different senses): "She lost her patience and her car keys." Rare. Max 1x per piece. Only when the double meaning genuinely surprises. Forced zeugma is worse than none.

**Anaphora** (repetition at sentence start): Creates emotional weight. "They fought for it. They bled for it. They lost." Max 1 set of 3 per piece. Use in kickers or key findings. Never in exposition.

**Anadiplosis** (end word becomes next start): Creates momentum. "The data pointed to fraud. Fraud on a scale nobody expected." Use in narrative passages where one idea drives the next.

**Parenthetical asides**: "(This matters because...)" or quiet caveats that add personality without disrupting flow. 2-3 per 1000 words. Use for genuine caveats, loopy details, or intellectual honesty. Not for decoration.

### Idiom & Colloquialism

Regional language grounds prose as human. AI defaults to formal, region-neutral phrasing.

**Rules:**
- 1-2 colloquialisms per 500 words in analytical prose
- Pick North American OR British English at run start. Maintain throughout. Never mix
- More colloquial in opinion/feature pieces, fewer in hard news/data reports

**North American set**: "in the weeds," "on the hook," "push back," "rethink the whole thing," "that's a stretch," "the numbers don't add up," "back of the envelope," "skin in the game," "baked in"

**British English set**: "it's a shambles," "rather," "quite," "I suspect," "no mean feat," "make a hash of it," "on the cards," "full stop," "spot on"

**Anti-patterns**: Never use cliched idioms from the kill list ("low-hanging fruit," "move the needle," "deep dive," "game changer"). Never introduce an idiom that needs explanation. If the reader has to pause, it failed.

**Bad** (region-neutral, formal):
> "The implementation encountered difficulties that were not anticipated by the planning team."

**Good** (North American, grounded):
> "The rollout hit a wall nobody saw coming. Three months in, the team was deep in the weeds with no clear way out."

### Syntactic Complexity Controls

AI generates denser subordination than humans: 0.75 dependent clauses per sentence vs. 0.57 for humans. This structural difference is measurable and hard to hide.

**Rules:**
- Max 1 dependent clause per sentence. If a sentence has 2+, split it
- Prefer prepositional phrases for relational embedding over explicit connectors

**Bad** (AI-typical, high subordination):
> "Because the cost increases in regions that have aging infrastructure, which requires additional investment that municipalities cannot afford, the federal government must intervene."

**Good** (human-typical, prepositional embedding):
> "The cost in regions with aging infrastructure keeps climbing. Municipalities can't absorb it. That leaves Ottawa."

- Flag any paragraph where 2+ sentences start with coordinating conjunctions (Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover). Replace one with a colon, period, or restructure
- Avoid stacking subordinate clauses. One "because" or "which" per sentence. The second one gets its own sentence

## 10-Pass Revision Protocol

Run sequentially on any prose before finalizing:

1. **Point-first**: Conclusion in the first sentence of every paragraph
2. **Kill-list**: Delete every banned word/phrase
3. **Verb**: Match every verb to the intensity ladder's magnitude
4. **Hedge**: Max one hedge per paragraph. Delete extras
5. **Voice**: Passive → active. "However/Therefore/Additionally" → "But/So/Also." Add contractions
6. **So-what**: Every statistic must answer "why should the reader care?"
7. **Rhythm**: Read aloud. Split tangles. Check sentence-length variety
8. **Selectivity**: Keep 2-3 data points per section. Cut or condense the rest
9. **Template-break**: If 3+ consecutive sentences use `[metric] [verb] [number]`, rewrite the third. No non-trivial word more than 5× per page
10. **AI-tell**: Zero em-dashes. Are contractions and short sentences placed where emphasis demands them, or uniformly sprinkled? Uniform distribution is itself a tell
11. **Human-signal audit**: Check burstiness (adjacent sentences differ by 5+ words? At least one fragment per 500 words?). Verify paragraph length variety (no two consecutive same-length). Confirm at least one rhetorical device (litotes, zeugma, anaphora, or parenthetical aside). Check for at least one colloquialism per 500 words. Ensure no three consecutive same-template paragraphs
12. **Register & audience check**: Verify the piece matches the audience profile set during intake. Check: sentence length within target range? Jargon level appropriate? Contraction frequency matches register? Evidence type matches audience expectations? If no intake was done, check against the general-public defaults. Flag any paragraph where the register shifts unexpectedly (formal → informal or vice versa)

## Integration with Data Analysis

When generating narrative text in data analysis projects:
1. Apply the data-analyst skill for chart/table/structure decisions
2. Apply this writing skill for all prose
3. Run the 12-pass revision protocol before finalizing
4. Cross-check: does every chart have a point-first description? Does every finding follow the three-forms rule?
