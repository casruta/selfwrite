## Key Learnings

1. **Pattern > conspiracy**: Op-eds that argue "pattern and consequence" are more persuasive than those arguing "strategy and intent." The reader doesn't need to believe in a coordinated plan to accept that the outcome is real.

2. **Concede, then redirect**: The strongest counterargument handling acknowledges motive ("Thiel may be purely anti-interventionist") then pivots to observable outcome ("but the cumulative effect..."). Dismissing intent ("intent is beside the point") reads as evasive.

3. **Cold reader review is highest-ROI**: The Clean Slate Review produced the largest single-step improvement (+0.55). Sourcing gaps, unexplained parentheticals, and vague referents are invisible to the writer but immediately obvious to a first-time reader.
