# The Quiet Checkbook Behind the GOP's Anti-Israel Turn

Peter Thiel doesn't run for office. He doesn't need to. The PayPal co-founder has spent the better part of a decade doing something far more effective: he's handpicked the candidates who do. And the candidates he picks keep ending up in the same ideological lane.

In the 2022 cycle alone, Thiel poured $35 million into 16 federal races, according to Federal Election Commission records. More than $15 million went to Protect Ohio Values, an independent super PAC backing JD Vance's Ohio Senate bid. Another $15 million bankrolled Blake Masters in Arizona. Both ran as America First populists. Both embraced Trump's election fraud claims. Both carried open contempt for the U.S.-Israel alliance, a stance the GOP establishment actively resisted until Thiel's candidates began normalizing it.

This isn't coincidence. It's a pattern. And the pattern has consequences.

Look at how the money works. Thiel's funding runs through layers built to hide its origins. Per Aspera Policy, a dark money nonprofit he controlled according to OpenSecrets and FEC filings, steered at least $200,000 into Vance's super PAC without disclosing a single donor. That sits on top of the $15 million Thiel gave directly. He also recruited roughly ten major donors for Vance, including venture capitalist David Sacks, who gave $1 million. A nonprofit shields the money trail. The super PAC spends it on ads. Voters see the ads. They never see who paid.

And then there's Joe Kent. An Army veteran with eleven combat tours and a CIA paramilitary background, Kent ran twice for Congress in Washington's 3rd District, merging Trump loyalty with deep skepticism of American military commitments abroad. Thiel backed him with direct donations at the $5,800 legal maximum per cycle. Kent lost both races to Democrat Marie Gluesenkamp Perez. Losing didn't end his career. Trump, who shared Kent's instinct for challenging foreign policy orthodoxy, appointed him director of the National Counterterrorism Center in 2025.

Then came March 2026. Kent resigned over the Iran war, calling it "manufactured." He blamed "high-ranking Israeli officials" for a "misinformation campaign" that drew America into conflict. He called the Syrian civil war (the conflict in which his wife, Navy cryptologist Shannon Kent, was killed by a suicide bomber in 2019) "a war manufactured by Israel." Senator McConnell responded by calling the letter "virulent antisemitism." The ADL cited Kent's "history of antisemitism and extremism."

Let's be honest about what this adds up to. Thiel funds candidates who challenge the Republican establishment's pro-Israel consensus. Those candidates win primaries, lose general elections, and still ascend to positions of power. The money flows through vehicles that don't disclose their sources. The candidates push a foreign policy that treats Israel not as an ally but as a liability.

Here's the pushback. Thiel is a libertarian who opposes foreign intervention broadly, not Israel specifically. His candidates are anti-war, not antisemitic. And his 2024 pullback (just $1.7 million in FEC filings, compared to $35 million two years earlier) suggests waning interest, not a long game.

Thiel's motives may be purely anti-interventionist. But the cumulative effect of his funding has reshaped the GOP's Israel consensus regardless. Vance, now vice president, has publicly questioned the scale of U.S. aid to Israel. Kent held one of the most sensitive counterterrorism posts in the federal government until he resigned in protest of a war he blamed squarely on Israeli influence. These are not fringe voices. They are the party's foreign policy mainstream now, and the money that put them there is largely untraceable.

This isn't about one billionaire's political hobby. It's about a party remaking its foreign policy from the inside, bankrolled by money voters can't trace. The real question is one voters deserve answered: who's paying, and why no receipts?
