# The Quiet Checkbook Behind the GOP's Anti-Israel Turn

Peter Thiel doesn't run for office. He doesn't need to. The PayPal co-founder and billionaire venture capitalist has spent the better part of a decade doing something more effective: handpicking the candidates who do.

In the 2022 cycle alone, Thiel poured $35 million into 16 federal races. More than $15 million went to a single super PAC, Protect Ohio Values, backing JD Vance's Senate bid. Another $15 million went to Blake Masters in Arizona. Both men ran as America First populists. Both embraced Trump's election claims. And both carried a foreign policy view that, until recently, would have been unthinkable in the Republican mainstream: skepticism, bordering on hostility, toward the U.S.-Israel alliance.

This isn't coincidence. It's strategy.

Thiel's funding network operates through layers designed to obscure its origins. Per Aspera Policy, a dark money nonprofit Thiel controlled, steered at least $200,000 into Vance's super PAC without disclosing its donors. That $200,000 sits on top of the $15 million Thiel gave directly. The structure is textbook: a nonprofit shields the money trail while the super PAC spends it on ads. The public sees the ads. They don't see who paid for them.

Joe Kent fits the same mold. An Army veteran with eleven combat tours and a CIA paramilitary background, Kent ran twice for Congress in Washington's 3rd District on a platform that merged Trump loyalty with deep skepticism of American military entanglements abroad. Thiel backed him with direct donations at the legal maximum. Kent lost both times, but the investment paid off differently: Trump appointed him director of the National Counterterrorism Center in 2025.

Then came March 2026. Kent resigned over the Iran war, calling it "manufactured" and blaming "high-ranking Israeli officials" for a "misinformation campaign" that drew America into conflict. He called the Syrian civil war, in which his own wife was killed, "a war manufactured by Israel." Senator Mitch McConnell called the letter "virulent antisemitism." The Anti-Defamation League pointed to Kent's "history of antisemitism and extremism."

The pattern is hard to miss. Thiel funds candidates who challenge the Republican establishment's traditional pro-Israel consensus. Those candidates win primaries, lose general elections, and then get appointed to positions of power anyway. The money flows through super PACs and dark money nonprofits that don't have to say where their funding comes from. The candidates push a foreign policy that treats Israel as a liability rather than an ally.

The counterargument writes itself: Thiel is a libertarian who opposes foreign intervention broadly, not Israel specifically. His candidates are anti-war, not antisemitic. And his 2024 pullback, when he gave just $1.7 million compared to $35 million in 2022, suggests he's losing interest, not building a machine.

But the machine is already built. Vance is vice president. Kent held one of the most sensitive counterterrorism posts in the federal government. The candidates Thiel bankrolled in 2022 now occupy positions where their foreign policy views shape American strategy, not just campaign rhetoric. The dark money that put them there has done its work and left no fingerprints.

The Republican Party is remaking its foreign policy. The question voters should ask is simple: who is paying for it, and why can't we see the receipt?
