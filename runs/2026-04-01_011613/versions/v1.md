# The Quiet Checkbook Behind the GOP's Anti-Israel Turn

Peter Thiel doesn't run for office. He doesn't need to. The PayPal co-founder has spent the better part of a decade doing something more effective: handpicking the candidates who do. And the candidates he picks keep ending up in the same ideological lane.

In the 2022 cycle alone, Thiel poured $35 million into 16 federal races. More than $15 million went to a single super PAC, Protect Ohio Values, backing JD Vance's Senate bid. Another $15 million went to Blake Masters in Arizona. Both ran as America First populists. Both embraced Trump's election claims. Both carried a foreign policy view that would have been unthinkable in the Republican mainstream five years earlier: open hostility toward the U.S.-Israel alliance.

This isn't coincidence. It's a pattern. And the pattern has consequences.

Look at how the money works. Thiel's funding runs through layers built to hide its origins. Per Aspera Policy, a dark money nonprofit he controlled, steered at least $200,000 into Vance's super PAC. No donor disclosure required. That $200,000 sits on top of the $15 million Thiel gave directly. A nonprofit shields the money trail. The super PAC spends it on ads. Voters see the ads. They never see who paid.

Joe Kent fits the same mold. An Army veteran with eleven combat tours and a CIA paramilitary background, Kent ran twice for Congress in Washington's 3rd District, merging Trump loyalty with deep skepticism of American military commitments abroad. Thiel backed him with direct donations at the legal maximum. Kent lost both times. But losing didn't end his career. Trump appointed him director of the National Counterterrorism Center in 2025.

Then came March 2026. Kent resigned over the Iran war, calling it "manufactured." He blamed "high-ranking Israeli officials" for a "misinformation campaign" that drew America into conflict. He called the Syrian civil war, the one in which his own wife was killed, "a war manufactured by Israel." Senator McConnell called the letter "virulent antisemitism." The ADL pointed to Kent's "history of antisemitism and extremism."

Bluntly, the pattern is this: Thiel funds candidates who challenge the Republican establishment's pro-Israel consensus. Those candidates win primaries, lose general elections, and then get appointed to positions of power anyway. The money flows through vehicles that don't disclose their sources. The candidates push a foreign policy that treats Israel not as an ally but as a liability.

Here's the pushback. Thiel is a libertarian who opposes foreign intervention broadly, not Israel specifically. His candidates are anti-war, not antisemitic. And his 2024 pullback, when he gave just $1.7 million compared to $35 million two years earlier, suggests waning interest.

But intent is beside the point. Whether Thiel set out to reshape the GOP's Israel stance or simply bankrolled candidates whose anti-interventionism landed there, the outcome is identical. Vance is vice president. Kent held one of the most sensitive counterterrorism posts in the federal government until he resigned in protest of a war he blamed on Israel. The dark money that elevated both men has done its work and left no fingerprints.

The Republican Party is remaking its foreign policy from the inside. The real question is one voters deserve an answer to: who is paying for it, and why are the receipts missing?
